Notion’s free plan caps you at 1,000 database blocks. That sounds generous until you realize a single project with 20 tasks and a few custom fields eats 50 blocks. ClickUp’s free tier, by contrast, gives you unlimited tasks but locks time tracking behind a $7/user/month paywall. If you’re a small team that lives in Slack and needs to log hours, that’s a dealbreaker before you even start.

## What You’ll Actually Pay

Both tools lure you in with free plans, but the real costs emerge when you scale. ClickUp’s pricing is straightforward: $7/user/month for Pro, $12 for Business, and $19 for Enterprise. Notion’s paid tiers are cheaper at first glance—$8/user/month for Plus, $15 for Business—but the block limit on the free plan forces upgrades sooner. A 10-person team hitting the block ceiling will pay $80/month on Notion, while the same team on ClickUp’s Pro plan pays $70.

Here’s where it gets messy: ClickUp charges per member, but Notion charges per workspace *guest*. If you collaborate with freelancers or clients, Notion’s guest access can balloon your bill. ClickUp’s guest seats are free, but they’re read-only—no editing, no comments.

| Feature                | ClickUp (Pro)       | Notion (Business)   | Who Wins?          |
|------------------------|---------------------|---------------------|--------------------|
| Price per user/month   | $7                  | $15                 | ClickUp            |
| Free guest access      | Yes (read-only)     | No (paid guests)    | ClickUp            |
| Time tracking          | Yes                 | No (via integrations)| ClickUp            |
| Database block limit   | Unlimited           | 250k blocks         | ClickUp            |
| Offline mode           | Yes (mobile)        | No                  | ClickUp            |

## Where It Shines (and Where It Doesn’t)

### ClickUp: The Swiss Army Knife That Can Cut You
ClickUp’s strength is its structure. It’s built for teams that need to *enforce* workflows, not just document them. The hierarchy—Spaces > Folders > Lists > Tasks—is rigid but intentional. If you’re managing a marketing team with recurring campaigns, sprints, and approvals, ClickUp’s templates (like the "Agile Scrum" or "Content Calendar") save you hours of setup. The built-in time tracking is a lifesaver for agencies billing by the hour, and the "Everything View" lets you slice tasks by assignee, due date, or custom fields without jumping between tabs.

But that structure is also its weakness. ClickUp’s flexibility is a double-edged sword. The sheer number of features—Dashboards, Goals, Docs, Chat, Whiteboards—can overwhelm new users. I’ve seen teams waste weeks customizing ClickUp only to abandon it because no one could agree on a workflow. The mobile app is functional but clunky; offline mode exists, but syncing can take minutes if you’re on a spotty connection.

### Notion: The Blank Canvas That Demands Discipline
Notion’s appeal is its simplicity. It’s a database, a wiki, and a task manager rolled into one, with a clean interface that feels like a cross between Google Docs and Airtable. For teams that value documentation—like engineering or product teams—Notion’s ability to embed databases, wikis, and tasks in a single page is unmatched. The "Relation" and "Rollup" features let you link databases in ways ClickUp can’t. For example, you can create a "Projects" database that pulls in tasks, docs, and team members from other databases, all in one view.

The problem? Notion isn’t *really* a project management tool. It’s a knowledge base that *can* do project management if you’re willing to put in the work. There’s no native time tracking, no Gantt charts (without third-party integrations), and no built-in dependencies. If you’re running a complex project with multiple stakeholders, you’ll spend more time configuring Notion than actually managing the work. The mobile app is also a letdown—no offline mode, and the interface feels cramped on smaller screens.

## The Rough Edges

ClickUp’s biggest complaint isn’t its feature bloat—it’s performance. As of May 2024, ClickUp’s G2 rating sits at 4.7/5, but the most common negative reviews cite slow load times and occasional crashes, especially when handling large Spaces with thousands of tasks. The company has acknowledged this in their [official changelog](https://clickup.com/changelog), noting improvements to "backend infrastructure" in Q1 2024, but users on Reddit still report lag when filtering large task lists.

Notion’s block limit isn’t just a pricing issue—it’s a workflow killer. A single page with 10 embedded databases can hit the 1,000-block limit on the free plan *before* you add any tasks. Notion’s workaround? Archive old pages. But archiving doesn’t free up blocks—it just hides them. Teams end up deleting content they might need later, or paying for a plan they don’t fully use. Notion’s API also has a [documented limitation](https://developers.notion.com/docs/working-with-databases): you can’t query more than 100 database rows at once, which makes integrations with tools like Zapier or Make clunky.

## What the Marketing Pages Won’t Tell You

ClickUp’s "Automations" are powerful but capped at 100 runs/month on the free plan. That’s fine for a solo user, but a team of 5 will hit that limit in a week. Notion’s "Sync Blocks" (for embedding databases across pages) sound great until you realize they’re read-only—you can’t edit the original database from the synced copy, which breaks workflows for teams that need to update tasks in multiple places.

Notion’s community is vocal about one missing feature: **native subtasks**. You can create a "Tasks" database and nest tasks under a parent task, but it’s a manual workaround. ClickUp, meanwhile, supports subtasks natively, but they’re buried in the task view—you can’t see them in the main list view without expanding each task.

## Who Should Use What?

If you’re a **small team (1-10 people) with simple workflows**—like a content team tracking blog posts or a startup managing product launches—Notion’s flexibility and lower cost might be enough. But if you need time tracking, dependencies, or Gantt charts, you’ll outgrow it fast.

If you’re a **mid-sized team (10-50 people) with complex projects**—like an agency juggling multiple clients or a product team running sprints—ClickUp’s structure and features will save you time. Just be prepared to invest in training. The learning curve is real, but the payoff is worth it for teams that need to standardize workflows.

For **enterprise teams (50+ people)**, neither tool is perfect. ClickUp’s performance issues become more noticeable at scale, and Notion’s block limits force awkward workarounds. If you’re in this camp, you’re better off with a tool like Jira or Asana, or waiting for ClickUp’s Enterprise plan to mature.

Pick ClickUp if you need a tool that *forces* structure. Pick Notion if you want a tool that *allows* structure—but only if you’re willing to build it yourself. Neither is perfect, but one will fit your team’s discipline (or lack thereof) better than the other.
