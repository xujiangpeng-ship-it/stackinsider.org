Basecamp’s flat $15/user/month pricing looks like a steal until you realize it caps projects at 100—after that, you’re forced into the $299/month Pro Unlimited plan, no matter how small your team. Teamwork, meanwhile, starts at $5.99/user/month but quietly adds a $49/month minimum spend, so a 5-person team pays $99/month before even hitting the first feature gate. Neither is as transparent as it seems.

## What Sets It Apart

Basecamp’s strength is its refusal to complicate things. The entire app fits into six tools: To-dos, Message Board, Campfire (chat), Docs & Files, Schedule, and Automatic Check-ins. There’s no Gantt chart to configure, no resource heatmap to decipher—just a single view where everything lives. For teams that spend more time doing the work than managing the tool, this is a relief. The Message Board, for example, replaces endless email threads by keeping discussions attached to the project, not buried in inboxes. It’s not flashy, but it’s the closest thing to a digital workspace that doesn’t feel like a spreadsheet.

Teamwork, on the other hand, is built for teams that need to *adapt* the tool to their process, not the other way around. It includes Gantt charts, time tracking, workload management, and custom fields—features Basecamp deliberately omits. The workload planner is particularly useful for agencies or service teams; it shows who’s overbooked at a glance, something Basecamp can’t do without manual spreadsheets. Teamwork also lets you set task dependencies, which Basecamp lacks entirely. If your projects have hard deadlines or complex handoffs, this is a dealbreaker.

## Where It Shines (and Where It Doesn’t)

| Feature                | Basecamp                          | Teamwork                          | Who Needs It?                     |
|------------------------|-----------------------------------|-----------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Pricing**            | $15/user/month (100 project cap)  | $5.99/user/month ($49 min)        | Small teams vs. growing agencies  |
| **Task Dependencies**  | ❌ No                             | ✅ Yes                            | Teams with sequential workflows   |
| **Time Tracking**      | ❌ No (3rd-party only)            | ✅ Built-in                       | Billable-hour teams               |
| **Workload View**      | ❌ No                             | ✅ Yes                            | Managers juggling team capacity   |
| **Mobile App**         | ✅ Simple, offline mode           | ✅ Full-featured, no offline      | Field teams vs. office workers    |

Basecamp’s mobile app is a rare bright spot. It works offline, syncs when you’re back online, and strips away everything but the essentials. Teamwork’s mobile app, by contrast, is a scaled-down version of the desktop experience—useful, but not something you’d want to rely on for field work. On the other hand, Teamwork’s time tracking is built in, while Basecamp forces you to integrate with Harvest or Toggl, adding another tool (and cost) to your stack.

One thing neither tool handles well? Client collaboration. Basecamp’s client access is an afterthought—you can invite clients, but they see the same messy interface your team does. Teamwork’s client permissions are more granular, but both tools assume clients want to log into *your* system, not their own. For agencies, this often means exporting reports or screenshots to keep clients in the loop, which defeats the purpose of an all-in-one tool.

## The Rough Edges

Basecamp’s simplicity is its biggest limitation. The lack of subtasks, dependencies, or even a basic Kanban view means teams with complex workflows will hit a wall. There’s also no way to assign a task to multiple people—something even free tools like Trello allow. The 100-project cap on the $15/user plan is another gotcha; once you hit it, you’re forced into the $299/month Pro Unlimited plan, which is overkill for most small teams.

Teamwork’s interface, meanwhile, can feel cluttered. The sheer number of features—Gantt charts, time tracking, workload views, custom fields—means there’s a learning curve. The mobile app lacks offline mode, which is frustrating for teams that work on the go. And while Teamwork’s pricing starts lower, the $49/month minimum spend means it’s not as cheap as it looks for very small teams.

A less obvious friction point: Teamwork’s API is powerful but poorly documented. Several users on Reddit’s r/projectmanagement have reported struggling to pull custom reports or automate workflows because the API endpoints aren’t clearly explained. Basecamp’s API, by contrast, is simpler but more limited—it’s easier to use, but you can’t do as much with it.

## What Users Actually Say

On G2, Basecamp holds a 4.3/5 rating (as of June 2026), with users praising its simplicity but criticizing the lack of advanced features. Teamwork scores slightly higher at 4.4/5, with users highlighting the workload planner and time tracking but complaining about the steep learning curve. One recurring theme in community forums: Basecamp users love it until they outgrow it, while Teamwork users tolerate its complexity because it scales better.

## Which One Fits Your Team?

If you’re a small team that hates managing tools more than doing the work, Basecamp’s simplicity will feel like a breath of fresh air. It’s not for everyone—agencies, product teams, or anyone with complex dependencies will find it limiting—but for teams that just need a place to organize tasks and conversations, it’s hard to beat.

If you’re an agency, service team, or any group that needs to track time, manage workloads, or handle sequential tasks, Teamwork is the better choice. It’s more expensive and more complicated, but it gives you the flexibility to adapt the tool to your process, not the other way around. Just be prepared to invest time in setup and training.

For teams that fall somewhere in between—maybe you need dependencies but not time tracking, or workload views but not Gantt charts—neither tool is a perfect fit. In that case, you might be better off with something like ClickUp or Asana, which offer more modularity. But if you’re choosing between these two, pick Basecamp for simplicity, Teamwork for control.
