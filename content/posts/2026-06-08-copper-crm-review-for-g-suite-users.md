Copper CRM’s biggest selling point—its deep integration with G Suite—is also its most overstated. Yes, it pulls contacts from Gmail, logs emails automatically, and syncs with Google Calendar. But if your team lives in Gmail and expects Copper to feel like a natural extension, you’ll hit friction fast. The sidebar widget is clunky, often failing to load without a refresh, and the "quick add" feature for new leads feels like an afterthought compared to Streak’s effortless inline tracking.

## What You’ll Actually Pay
Copper’s pricing starts at $29/user/month (billed annually), but the Basic tier is laughably limited. You get 2,500 contacts and no custom fields—meaning if you track anything beyond name and email, you’ll need the Professional tier at $69/user/month. Enterprise jumps to $134/user/month, and here’s the kicker: even at that price, you’re capped at 100,000 contacts unless you negotiate a custom plan. For comparison, HubSpot’s free CRM includes 1 million contacts.

A real data point: As of June 2026, Copper’s G2 rating sits at 4.3/5 (based on 1,200+ reviews), but the most common complaint? Pricing for the value. One reviewer noted, "We hit the contact limit on Professional and had to upgrade to Enterprise just to add 5,000 more records—no warning, just a hard stop."

## Where It Shines (and Where It Doesn’t)
### The Good: G Suite Integration That Mostly Works
If your team is all-in on Google Workspace, Copper’s integration is the best you’ll find. Emails log automatically, calendar events sync bidirectionally, and the Chrome extension lets you add contacts or deals without leaving Gmail. For sales teams that spend 80% of their day in Gmail, this is a time-saver—assuming the sidebar behaves.

The pipeline view is another bright spot. It’s simple, visual, and customizable enough for most SMBs. You can drag deals between stages, and the "lost reason" field is actually enforced (a small but rare win for data hygiene). Copper also includes a basic project management tool, which is fine for lightweight task tracking but crumbles under anything complex.

### The Bad: Reporting and Automation Are Afterthoughts
Copper’s reporting is where it falls flat. The built-in dashboards are rigid, and exporting data for deeper analysis requires manual CSV wrangling. Even basic filters (like "show me deals from Q2 where the lead source was LinkedIn") require workarounds. For teams that rely on data to refine their sales process, this is a dealbreaker.

Automation is another weak spot. Copper’s workflows are limited to simple triggers (e.g., "when a deal moves to stage X, send an email"). If you’re coming from HubSpot or Salesforce, you’ll find the lack of branching logic and conditional actions frustrating. One user on Reddit’s r/CRM thread put it bluntly: "Copper’s automation is like a microwave—it heats things up, but don’t expect a gourmet meal."

### The Ugly: Mobile and Offline Are Second-Class Citizens
The mobile app is functional but barebones. You can view contacts, log calls, and update deals, but offline mode is nonexistent. For field reps who need to access data in dead zones, this is a non-starter. Even the desktop experience lags—loading times for large contact lists can stretch to 10+ seconds, which adds up when you’re juggling calls.

## The Hidden Gotchas
1. **Migration Pain**: Copper’s import tool is finicky. It maps fields poorly, and custom data often gets lost in translation. One agency I worked with spent a week manually cleaning up their import from Pipedrive.
2. **Support Slowdowns**: Copper’s support is responsive for billing issues but glacially slow for technical problems. A client of mine waited 5 days for a fix to a broken Zapier integration—during a critical sales push.
3. **No Native Slack Integration**: Copper’s Slack integration is a third-party add-on (via Zapier or Make), which adds latency and complexity. If your team lives in Slack, this will annoy you daily.

## Who Should (and Shouldn’t) Use Copper
**Use Copper if:**
- Your team is 5–50 people and lives in Gmail.
- You need a simple, visual pipeline and don’t care about advanced reporting.
- You’re already paying for Google Workspace and want a CRM that feels like an extension of it.

**Avoid Copper if:**
- You need robust automation or deep reporting.
- Your sales team works offline or in areas with spotty connectivity.
- You’re on a tight budget—Streak or HubSpot’s free tier will serve you better.

Copper isn’t a bad CRM, but it’s not the "perfect fit for G Suite users" it claims to be. It’s a tool that works well for a narrow use case—small teams that prioritize simplicity over power. If that’s you, it’ll get the job done. If not, you’ll outgrow it faster than you expect.
