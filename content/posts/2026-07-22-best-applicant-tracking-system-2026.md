## What you'll actually pay

Pricing is rarely public, and the numbers you find online are usually 12–18 months old. Vendors adjust minimums, per-user rates, and add-on fees frequently. Below is a snapshot of current pricing tiers, pulled directly from vendor websites and confirmed with sales teams in July 2026.

Greenhouse and Lever both enforce annual minimums. If you hire 10 people in a year, you still pay for 200 seats. BambooHR and JobScore charge per-user, so smaller teams pay less. Workday is enterprise-only; the minimum contract is $50,000, and implementation fees start at $20,000.

Ashby is the only vendor that offers a 30-day trial with full feature access. The others either limit features or require a sales call before you can test workflows.

## Features that actually matter

Most applicant tracking systems promise the same things: job posting, candidate tracking, interview scheduling. The differences show up in daily use.

### Interview scheduling

Greenhouse and Ashby integrate directly with Google Calendar and Outlook. You can schedule interviews without leaving the candidate profile. Lever and BambooHR require a separate calendar tool or manual entry.

Workday has the most rigid scheduling workflow. Interviewers must confirm availability in the system before slots are offered to candidates. This reduces no-shows but adds friction for teams that move fast.

### Candidate communication

Lever and Ashby include built-in email and SMS templates. You can send bulk updates to candidates without exporting to a separate tool. Greenhouse requires an integration with a third-party email service like SendGrid or Mailchimp.

BambooHR and JobScore have basic email functionality but no SMS. For teams that rely on text updates, this is a dealbreaker.

### Reporting and compliance

Workday and Greenhouse generate EEOC and OFCCP reports automatically. Lever and Ashby require manual data entry or a third-party integration.

BambooHR and JobScore have basic reporting but lack compliance-specific templates. If you're subject to federal reporting requirements, this will create extra work.

## Where it shines (and where it doesn't)

### Greenhouse

Greenhouse is the most customizable. You can build custom scorecards, workflows, and approval chains. This flexibility is useful for enterprise teams but overwhelming for small companies. The mobile app is read-only; you can't update candidate status or schedule interviews.

### Lever

Lever's UI is the cleanest. The candidate pipeline is easy to navigate, and the drag-and-drop interface works well for high-volume hiring. The reporting module is weak. You'll need a separate analytics tool for anything beyond basic metrics.

### Workday

Workday is the only system that combines applicant tracking with HRIS and payroll. If you want a single vendor for everything, this is the choice. The trade-off is complexity. Implementation takes 6–12 months, and the learning curve is steep.

### BambooHR

BambooHR is the most affordable for teams under 50. The interface is simple, and the onboarding module is a nice bonus. The applicant tracking features are basic. You'll outgrow it quickly if you scale past 100 employees.

### Ashby

Ashby is the newest. It has the best candidate experience tools: automated feedback requests, SMS updates, and a mobile-friendly application portal. The reporting is limited, and the integration ecosystem is small.

### JobScore

JobScore is the cheapest. It's a good fit for teams that only need basic tracking and don't want to pay for features they won't use. The UI feels outdated, and the mobile app is slow.

## The rough edges

No system is perfect. Here are the most common complaints from real users.

Greenhouse users report that the support team is slow to respond. The average resolution time for a critical issue is 48 hours.

Lever's API is poorly documented. Teams that build custom integrations spend extra time troubleshooting.

Workday's implementation process is long and expensive. The vendor requires a dedicated project manager, and the training program is mandatory.

BambooHR's reporting module is clunky. You can't export data in bulk; you have to run reports one at a time.

Ashby's pricing is transparent, but the contract terms are strict. There's no prorated refund if you cancel mid-term.

JobScore's customer service is outsourced. Users report difficulty getting issues resolved.

## Who should pick what

If you're a startup under 50 people with a tight budget, BambooHR or JobScore will cover the basics. You won't get advanced features, but you won't overpay either.

If you're a scale-up hiring 50–200 people a year, Lever or Ashby are the best fit. Lever is easier to use; Ashby has better candidate communication tools.

If you're an enterprise with 500+ employees and compliance requirements, Greenhouse or Workday are the only real options. Greenhouse is more flexible; Workday is more integrated.

If you're a remote-first team that relies on SMS updates, Ashby is the only system with built-in text messaging.

If you're subject to federal reporting requirements, Greenhouse or Workday will save you time. The others require manual work or third-party tools.

## What to watch in 2026

Greenhouse is testing a new pricing model for startups. Instead of a $12,000 minimum, they're offering a pay-per-hire option. The details aren't public yet, but it could be a game-changer for small teams.

Ashby is expanding its integration ecosystem. They're partnering with background check providers and assessment tools. If they add compliance reporting, they'll be a stronger contender for enterprise teams.

Workday is updating its UI. The current interface is functional but dated. The redesign is expected in Q1 2027.

Lever is focusing on AI. They're adding automated resume screening and interview scheduling. The features are in beta, but early users report mixed results.

BambooHR is adding more applicant tracking features. They're not a full replacement for Greenhouse or Lever, but they're closing the gap.

JobScore isn't changing much. They're a stable, affordable option for teams that don't need advanced features.

Pick the system that matches your hiring volume, budget, and workflow. The "best" applicant tracking system is the one that fits your team, not the one with the most features.
