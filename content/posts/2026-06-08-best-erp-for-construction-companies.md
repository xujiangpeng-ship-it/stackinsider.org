You’re probably here because your current system—whether it’s QuickBooks plus spreadsheets or a clunky legacy ERP—is costing you more in manual work than it saves in automation. The right ERP for construction doesn’t just track costs; it *prevents* the kind of budget overruns that sink projects before they’re even 30% complete. But most vendors won’t tell you that their "industry-specific" features are bolted onto generic accounting software, or that their mobile app is useless offline when your crew is on-site.

Here’s the truth: there’s no one-size-fits-all ERP for construction, but there are clear winners depending on your team size, budget, and whether you prioritize job costing, equipment tracking, or compliance. Let’s cut through the noise and focus on what actually moves the needle.

## What Sets It Apart (and Where It Doesn’t)

### Job Costing That Doesn’t Require a PhD
Most ERPs claim to handle job costing, but few do it in a way that superintendents or project managers can actually *use* without calling IT. **Procore** and **Viewpoint Vista** stand out here—not because they’re perfect, but because they let field teams code costs to the right phase or cost code *without* navigating five screens. Procore’s mobile app, for example, lets foremen snap a photo of a delivery receipt and assign it to a cost code in under 30 seconds. That’s the difference between data that’s *collected* and data that’s *actionable*.

**Viewpoint Vista** takes it a step further with its "unit cost" tracking, which is a godsend for highway or utility contractors who bill by the foot, yard, or ton. You can set up formulas to automatically calculate production rates and flag variances—something most ERPs force you to do in Excel. The catch? Vista’s interface looks like it was designed in 2005, and the learning curve is steep. If your team is resistant to change, this will be a hard sell.

### Equipment Tracking That Actually Prevents Downtime
Equipment is the second-biggest cost center for most contractors, but most ERPs treat it as an afterthought. **Foundation Software’s FOUNDATION** and **Deltek + ComputerEase** are the exceptions. FOUNDATION’s equipment module lets you track maintenance schedules, fuel usage, and even operator certifications in one place. It’ll alert you when a piece of equipment is due for service *and* show you the cost of downtime if you ignore it. That’s the kind of proactive insight that saves six figures a year for mid-sized contractors.

Deltek + ComputerEase goes deeper with its integration to **Dexter + Chaney’s Spectrum**, which includes GPS tracking and telematics data from providers like **Geotab**. You can see where your equipment is, how many hours it’s idling, and whether it’s being used on the job it’s billed to. The downside? The setup is complex, and you’ll need a dedicated equipment manager to get the most out of it. If you’re a smaller contractor with a handful of skid steers, this is overkill.

### The Mobile App Reality Check
Here’s the dirty secret about construction ERPs: most of their mobile apps are glorified web browsers with limited offline functionality. **Procore’s mobile app** is the best of the bunch—it works offline, syncs when you’re back in range, and even lets you submit RFIs or punch lists from the field. But it’s not perfect. Users report that the app crashes when uploading large photos or videos, and the navigation isn’t intuitive for crews who aren’t tech-savvy.

**Autodesk Construction Cloud (ACC)** has a slick mobile app, but it’s more of a project management tool than a full ERP. If you’re already using **BIM 360** or **PlanGrid**, ACC’s mobile experience is a natural extension. But if you need deep job costing or equipment tracking on the go, you’ll be disappointed. **Sage 100 Contractor** and **Viewpoint Spectrum** have mobile apps, but they’re essentially read-only. You can view data, but don’t expect to enter timecards or approve change orders from the field.

## What You’ll Actually Pay

Pricing for construction ERPs is notoriously opaque, but here’s what you need to know: **most vendors charge per user *and* per module**, which means costs can spiral quickly. Here’s a breakdown of what you’re looking at for a mid-sized contractor (50-100 employees):

| ERP               | Starting Price (Annual) | Key Pricing Notes                                                                 |
|-------------------|-------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Procore           | $50,000+                | Priced per project, not per user. $50k covers ~10 active projects.                |
| Viewpoint Vista   | $30,000+                | Requires on-premise server or cloud hosting (add $10k+/year).                     |
| Foundation        | $20,000+                | Includes job costing and equipment tracking out of the box.                       |
| Deltek + ComputerEase | $25,000+           | Pricing varies by module. Equipment tracking adds $5k+/year.                      |
| Sage 100 Contractor | $15,000+            | Cheaper upfront, but add-ons (like payroll) add up.                               |

**Procore’s pricing model** is unique—it’s based on the number of active projects, not users. That’s great if you have a small team managing many projects, but it’s a nightmare if you have a large team working on a few big jobs. For example, a $50 million highway project could cost you $20k/year in Procore fees alone. **Viewpoint Vista** and **Foundation** charge per user, which is more predictable but can get expensive if you have a lot of field staff.

**Hidden Costs to Watch For:**
- **Implementation fees**: Most vendors charge 50-100% of the annual license fee for setup. Procore’s implementation starts at $25k, and Viewpoint Vista’s can exceed $50k for complex setups.
- **Training**: Expect to pay $1k-$3k per day for on-site training. Remote training is cheaper but less effective.
- **Third-party integrations**: Need to connect to your payroll provider or CRM? That’ll cost extra. Procore’s API access starts at $10k/year.
- **Upgrades**: On-premise solutions like Viewpoint Vista require IT staff to manage upgrades. Cloud solutions like Procore include upgrades, but you’ll pay for new features.

## The Rough Edges

### Integration Friction No One Talks About
Most ERPs claim to "integrate seamlessly" with other tools, but the reality is messier. **Procore’s integration with Sage 300 CRE** is a common pain point. Users report that data syncs are slow, and cost codes often mismatch between the two systems. If you’re using Procore for project management and Sage for accounting, you’ll need a dedicated person to reconcile the two.

**Viewpoint Vista’s API** is powerful but requires custom development. If you want to connect it to your payroll system or CRM, you’ll need to hire a developer or pay Viewpoint’s professional services team. **Foundation’s integrations** are more plug-and-play, but they’re limited to a handful of partners like **QuickBooks** and **HCSS**.

### The Compliance Black Hole
Construction is one of the most regulated industries, but most ERPs treat compliance as an afterthought. **Deltek + ComputerEase** is the exception—it includes built-in compliance tracking for **Davis-Bacon**, **OSHA**, and **AIA billing**. But even here, users report that the compliance reports are clunky and require manual adjustments.

**Procore’s compliance features** are basic. You can track certifications and safety incidents, but don’t expect it to handle prevailing wage calculations or union reporting. If compliance is a major concern, you’ll need a dedicated tool like **LCPtracker** or **eCompliance** alongside your ERP.

### The Migration Nightmare
Migrating to a new ERP is like moving your entire office while still trying to get work done. **Procore’s migration tools** are the best in the industry—they’ll import your data from QuickBooks, Sage, or Viewpoint with minimal fuss. But even with Procore, expect to spend 3-6 months cleaning up your data and training your team.

**Viewpoint Vista’s migration process** is notoriously painful. Users report that data often gets lost or corrupted during the transfer, and Viewpoint’s support team is slow to respond. If you’re coming from a legacy system like **Timberline**, budget extra time and money for the transition.

## Where It Falls Short (And What to Use Instead)

### For Small Contractors (Under 50 Employees)
Most ERPs are overkill for small contractors. If you’re using QuickBooks and spreadsheets, **Foundation** or **Sage 100 Contractor** are the best next steps. Foundation is easier to use and includes equipment tracking out of the box, while Sage 100 is more affordable but requires add-ons for job costing.

**Avoid Procore** unless you’re managing multiple large projects. The cost isn’t worth it for small teams.

### For Mid-Sized Contractors (50-200 Employees)
This is where the competition gets fierce. **Procore** and **Viewpoint Vista** are the top contenders, but they serve different needs:
- **Procore** if you prioritize project management and collaboration. It’s the best tool for GCs and subcontractors who need to manage RFIs, submittals, and punch lists in one place.
- **Viewpoint Vista** if you need deep job costing and equipment tracking. It’s the better choice for self-performing contractors who bill by unit cost.

**Foundation** is a good middle ground if you want a simpler, more affordable option, but it lacks the scalability of Procore or Vista.

### For Large Contractors (200+ Employees)
**Deltek + ComputerEase** and **Viewpoint Vista** are the only ERPs that can handle the complexity of large contractors. Deltek is the better choice if you need **BIM integration** or **global compliance tracking**, while Vista is stronger for **heavy civil** or **utility contractors**.

**Avoid Sage 100 Contractor**—it’s not scalable enough for large teams.

## The Verdict

If you’re a **general contractor** managing multiple projects and need a tool that your field teams will actually use, **Procore** is the best choice—despite the cost. It’s not perfect, but it’s the closest thing to a "one-stop shop" for construction project management.

If you’re a **self-performing contractor** (e.g., highway, utility, or concrete) and job costing is your top priority, **Viewpoint Vista** is the way to go. Just be prepared for a steep learning curve and a UI that feels outdated.

If you’re a **small to mid-sized contractor** on a budget, **Foundation** is the best balance of affordability and functionality. It’s not as flashy as Procore, but it gets the job done without breaking the bank.

And if you’re a **large contractor** with complex needs, **Deltek + ComputerEase** is the only ERP that can handle it all—but expect to pay for the privilege.

The right ERP won’t solve all your problems, but the wrong one will create new ones. Choose based on what your team *actually* needs, not what the sales rep promises.
