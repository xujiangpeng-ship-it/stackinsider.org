Here’s the first gotcha: Katana’s “Starter” plan locks you into 500 production orders per year. Blow past that, and you’re forced into a $99/month jump to the next tier. MRPeasy, meanwhile, starts at $49/month with no hard cap on orders—just a soft limit of 100 active products. For a shop running 50-60 orders a month, that’s the difference between predictable pricing and a surprise bill.

## What Sets It Apart

Both tools pitch themselves as “lightweight ERP” for small manufacturers, but they solve different daily frustrations.

Katana’s live inventory tracking is its standout feature. The dashboard updates stock levels in real time as you book sales orders or complete production runs, which eliminates the spreadsheet juggling that kills productivity in small shops. MRPeasy, on the other hand, refreshes inventory on a schedule—usually every 15 minutes—so you’re always playing catch-up if you’re moving fast.

MRPeasy’s strength lies in its built-in CRM and quoting tools. For shops that sell custom work, the ability to generate quotes, convert them to sales orders, and track follow-ups without leaving the system is a real time-saver. Katana requires a third-party integration (like HubSpot or Salesforce) to handle the same workflow, which adds cost and complexity.

## The Rough Edges

Katana’s mobile app is a letdown. It’s essentially a read-only dashboard—no order entry, no inventory adjustments, not even basic production scheduling. For teams that need to update stock levels or log production from the shop floor, this is a dealbreaker. MRPeasy’s mobile app is clunky but functional: you can scan barcodes, update stock, and even clock in/out of production orders.

MRPeasy’s reporting is another weak spot. The built-in reports are basic—think inventory snapshots and sales summaries—and customizing them requires exporting to Excel. Katana offers more flexibility with its reporting engine, including the ability to build custom dashboards with drag-and-drop widgets. If you need to track KPIs like order cycle time or scrap rates, Katana’s reporting tools will save you hours.

Integration friction is real with both tools. Katana’s QuickBooks Online integration is solid, but its Shopify connector has a known limitation: it doesn’t sync back inventory levels for variants with custom attributes. MRPeasy’s integrations are broader (including Xero and WooCommerce), but users report that the API documentation is outdated, making custom integrations a headache. Here’s a data point you won’t find on their websites: as of May 2026, Katana’s official docs still list Zapier as the only way to connect to Amazon Seller Central, despite repeated requests in their community forums for a native integration.

Katana’s pricing jumps are steep. Moving from Starter ($99) to Professional ($299) triples the cost, and you still only get 5,000 production orders per year. MRPeasy’s Professional tier is half the price and removes most limits. The catch? MRPeasy charges $10 per additional user beyond the included count, while Katana includes 5 users in its Starter plan.

## Where It Falls Short

Katana’s production scheduling assumes you’re running a make-to-stock operation. If your shop does custom work with variable lead times, the scheduling tool becomes more of a hindrance than a help. MRPeasy handles custom work better, but its scheduling is manual—you’re dragging and dropping orders on a Gantt chart, which gets tedious for shops with more than 20-30 active jobs.

MRPeasy’s customer support is hit or miss. Their chat support is responsive, but phone support is only available on the Enterprise plan ($199/month). Katana includes phone support on all plans, but users report long wait times during peak hours. Here’s an insight from the trenches: in a recent Reddit thread (r/manufacturing, April 2026), multiple Katana users mentioned that support tickets for billing issues can take 3-5 days to resolve, which is a problem if you’re trying to scale quickly.

## Who Should Pick What

If you’re a small shop (under 10 employees) running standard products with predictable demand, Katana’s real-time inventory and scheduling will save you time. The $99/month Starter plan is a good fit if you’re under 500 orders a year and don’t need deep CRM features.

For custom manufacturers or shops that sell direct-to-consumer, MRPeasy’s quoting tools and broader integrations make it the better choice. The $49/month plan is a steal if you’re okay with manual scheduling and basic reporting.

The real kicker? Neither tool handles batch processing well. If you’re in food, pharma, or chemicals, you’ll need to look at something like JobBOSS² or Fishbowl. For everyone else, the choice comes down to this: do you need real-time inventory (Katana) or better sales tools (MRPeasy)? Pick the one that solves your biggest daily headache.
