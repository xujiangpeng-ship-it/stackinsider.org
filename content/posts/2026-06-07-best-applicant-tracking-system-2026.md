Ashby’s pricing is the most transparent: $40 per user/month, with add-ons clearly listed in their public docs. Greenhouse and Lever, by contrast, bury the “Advanced” modules in sales conversations. Workday’s flat fee is predictable but overkill for teams under 200.

## Features That Actually Matter

### The One-Click Sourcing Hack
Ashby’s Chrome extension lets recruiters add candidates to a pipeline with a single click—no tab-switching, no copy-paste errors. Greenhouse has a similar tool, but it only works on LinkedIn; Ashby supports 12 job boards, including niche sites like AngelList and Wellfound. For startups hiring engineers, this is the difference between filling a role in 14 days or 30.

### The Interview Scorecard That Doesn’t Suck
Lever’s scorecards auto-populate from LinkedIn profiles, but they’re rigid: you can’t customize fields per role. Greenhouse lets you build role-specific scorecards, but the UI is cluttered—recruiters complain it takes 5 clicks to submit feedback. Ashby’s scorecards strike a balance: drag-and-drop customization with one-click submission.

### The Reporting Black Hole
Workday’s analytics are powerful but require a dedicated data analyst to set up. Greenhouse’s reports are pre-built but lack granularity—you can’t filter by candidate source *and* hiring manager. Ashby’s reporting is the most flexible: you can create custom dashboards with drag-and-drop widgets, and it integrates natively with Power BI and Tableau.

## The Rough Edges

### Greenhouse: The Integration Tax
Greenhouse’s API is robust, but it’s not plug-and-play. Their docs admit that “custom integrations require a dedicated engineer for setup and maintenance.” For teams without in-house devs, this means relying on third-party tools like Zapier, which adds $500–$1,500/year to the total cost.

### Lever: The Mobile Gap
Lever’s mobile app hasn’t seen a major update since 2023. Users report it crashes when uploading resumes from iOS files, and the interview scheduling feature is read-only. For recruiters who need to review candidates on the go, this is a dealbreaker.

### Ashby: The Learning Curve
Ashby’s UI is clean, but its automation rules are complex. Setting up a workflow to auto-reject candidates without a specific keyword requires navigating a nested menu system. Greenhouse and Lever offer simpler rule builders, though with fewer customization options.

### Workday: The Overkill Problem
Workday is the only tool here with native payroll and HRIS, but that’s also its downside. The ATS module is buried in a sprawling interface, and recruiters complain it takes 3–4 clicks to get to the hiring dashboard. For teams that don’t need an all-in-one HR suite, it’s like buying a Ferrari to drive to the grocery store.

## What the Marketing Pages Won’t Tell You

### The Hidden Cost of “Free” Job Board Postings
Greenhouse and Lever advertise “free job board postings,” but this only applies to their basic integrations. Posting to premium boards like Indeed or Glassdoor requires a paid add-on ($500–$1,500/year). Ashby includes these in its base plan.

### The Candidate Experience Trade-Off
Greenhouse’s application forms are customizable but slow to load—candidates report 3–5 second delays on mobile. Ashby’s forms are faster but offer fewer branding options. If candidate drop-off is a concern, test both with a real job posting before committing.

### The Migration Headache
Migrating from Lever to Greenhouse takes 4–6 weeks, according to a 2025 G2 review. Ashby’s migration tool is faster (2–3 weeks), but it doesn’t transfer interview feedback or scorecards—only candidate data. Workday’s migration is seamless if you’re already using their HRIS, but for everyone else, it’s a 3-month project.

## Which One Fits Your Team?

- **Startups (10–50 employees, <$10k budget)**: Ashby. The pricing is transparent, the sourcing tools save time, and the reporting is flexible enough to grow with you. Skip the “Advanced” add-ons unless you’re scaling fast.
- **Mid-market (50–500 employees, $10k–$25k budget)**: Greenhouse. The customization options justify the cost, but budget for the “Advanced Onboarding” module upfront. If you’re already using Workday for HR, stick with their ATS—even if it’s overkill.
- **Enterprise (500+ employees, $25k+ budget)**: Workday. The all-in-one HR suite is worth the complexity if you’re managing global hiring. For teams that only need an ATS, Lever’s analytics are a better fit, but expect to pay for the “Advanced” tier.

If you’re hiring for high-volume roles (e.g., retail, customer support), test Ashby’s automation rules first—it’s the only tool here that lets you auto-reject candidates based on custom criteria without manual review. For niche roles (e.g., data scientists, designers), Greenhouse’s custom scorecards are worth the extra cost.
