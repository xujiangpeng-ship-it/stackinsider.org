## What small businesses actually need from HR software

Most small teams don’t need AI-powered talent analytics or global compliance modules. They need software that handles three things without creating extra work:

1. Payroll that doesn’t require a CPA to run.
2. Onboarding that stops new hires from asking, “What’s my login again?”
3. Time-off tracking that doesn’t turn into a spreadsheet nightmare.

Everything else is optional. The problem is that most HR platforms treat these basics as loss leaders to upsell you into their enterprise suite.

## Where each tool shines (and where it doesn’t)

### Gusto: The payroll specialist

Gusto handles payroll well enough that I’ve seen teams drop their external accountant. The automated tax filings work for most states, and the mobile app lets employees access pay stubs without bugging HR. But the onboarding flow feels dated. New hires still get a PDF packet to sign, and the system doesn’t auto-generate logins for other tools like Slack or Google Workspace.

The biggest frustration is the pricing structure. The $40 base fee is manageable for a 10-person team, but it jumps to $80 for 20 employees before you even add benefits or time tracking. If you’re growing fast, those costs add up quickly.

### Rippling: The modular headache

Rippling’s pitch is simple: pick only the modules you need. In practice, this means you’ll pay for payroll, benefits, and time tracking separately. A 15-person team can easily hit $300/month once you add everything. The integration between modules is smooth, but the pricing isn’t transparent. I’ve had clients get sticker shock when the invoice arrives because the website doesn’t show the full cost upfront.

The onboarding is the best in class. Rippling auto-provisions accounts for email, Slack, and even IT equipment. But if you don’t need that level of automation, you’re overpaying for features you won’t use.

### BambooHR: The HR-focused option

BambooHR does one thing well: HR. The interface is clean, and the reporting tools are more flexible than Gusto’s. But it doesn’t do payroll, so you’ll need to integrate with another tool like ADP or Gusto. That means managing two systems and double the login credentials.

The pricing is high for what you get. At $61 base + $11 per employee, a 20-person team pays $281/month just for HR features. If you’re not using the advanced reporting, it’s hard to justify the cost.

### Deel: The global option (with trade-offs)

Deel is the only tool on this list that handles international contractors and employees well. The compliance features are strong, and the platform supports payments in multiple currencies. But it’s expensive. The base fee is $49, and payroll adds another $49 + $12 per employee. For a small business with a few remote workers, it’s overkill.

The interface also feels cluttered. The dashboard tries to show everything at once, which makes it hard to find what you need. If you’re only hiring in one country, another tool will be simpler and cheaper.

### Homebase: The budget pick

Homebase is the only tool here with a free tier. It covers scheduling, time tracking, and basic HR for up to 20 employees. The payroll add-on is affordable at $39 + $6 per employee, but it’s only available in 12 states. If you’re outside those states, you’ll need to use another payroll provider.

The mobile app is surprisingly good. Employees can clock in, request time off, and view schedules from their phones. But the HR features are limited. You won’t get onboarding workflows or advanced reporting.

## What you’ll actually pay

Most small businesses underestimate the total cost of HR software. Here’s what to budget beyond the base price:

- Payroll add-ons: $39–$89/month + $6–$12 per employee.
- Benefits administration: $5–$10 per employee.
- Time tracking: $2–$6 per employee.
- Implementation fees: $200–$1,000 (common with Rippling and BambooHR).

A 15-person team using Gusto with payroll and time tracking will pay around $250/month. The same setup with Rippling could cost $400 or more.

## The rough edges

No HR software is perfect. Here’s what users complain about most in 2026:

- Gusto: Customer support response times have gotten worse since the private equity buyout. Simple issues can take days to resolve.
- Rippling: The mobile app is slow and crashes frequently. Employees struggle to clock in or request time off.
- BambooHR: The reporting tools are powerful but require training. Most small teams don’t use them.
- Deel: The compliance features are strong, but the platform feels bloated. Simple tasks like running payroll take too many clicks.
- Homebase: The free tier is great, but the payroll add-on is limited to a few states. Most teams will need to integrate with another tool.

## Who should pick what

If you’re a small business with 5–20 employees in the U.S., Gusto is the safest choice. The payroll is reliable, and the pricing is predictable. Skip the add-ons unless you really need them.

If you’re growing fast and need automation, Rippling is worth the cost. Just be prepared for sticker shock when the invoice arrives.

If you’re hiring internationally, Deel is the only real option. But if you’re only hiring in one country, another tool will be simpler and cheaper.

If you’re on a tight budget, Homebase is the best free option. Just don’t expect advanced features.

BambooHR is the hardest to recommend. The pricing is high, and you’ll still need a separate payroll provider. Unless you need the advanced reporting, another tool will serve you better.

## What to watch in 2026

Gusto is rumored to be launching a global payroll product later this year. If that happens, it could make Deel less necessary for small businesses with remote teams. Rippling is also expanding its IT and finance modules, which could make it a better fit for tech-heavy teams.

For now, most small businesses should stick with Gusto or Homebase. The others are either too expensive or too complex for what small teams actually need.
