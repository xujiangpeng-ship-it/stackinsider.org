Most ecommerce accounting tools promise the same thing: sync orders, track inventory, reconcile payouts. The reality is messier. I’ve helped teams migrate from spreadsheets to dedicated tools, and the biggest headache isn’t the software itself—it’s the gaps between what the marketing says and what your store actually needs.

Here’s what matters when you’re choosing accounting software for ecommerce, based on real implementations.

## What you’ll actually pay

Pricing for ecommerce accounting tools isn’t just about the monthly fee. The real costs show up in three places:

1. Transaction volume. Tools like QuickBooks Online charge per bank connection and transaction sync. If you process 10,000 orders a month, that $30 plan can jump to $100 or more.
2. Add-ons. Shopify stores often need the QuickBooks Commerce add-on for $20/month to sync inventory properly. Xero’s ecommerce integrations require a separate app like A2X, which starts at $19/month.
3. Migration. Moving from spreadsheets or another tool often means hiring a bookkeeper for a few hours. I’ve seen teams spend $500–$1,500 just to clean up historical data before importing.

Zoho Books is a good middle ground, but its reporting isn’t as flexible as Xero’s.

## Features that actually matter

### Reconciling payouts vs. orders

Ecommerce stores don’t get paid per order. You get a lump payout from Shopify, Amazon, or PayPal, and those payouts include fees, refunds, and sometimes multiple orders. The tool you choose needs to match those payouts to the original orders automatically.

A2X does this well for Xero and QuickBooks, but it’s an extra cost. Without it, you’re manually matching deposits to orders, which takes hours every month. I’ve seen stores with 500+ orders a month spend 10+ hours reconciling payouts because their accounting tool didn’t handle this well.

### Inventory tracking that doesn’t break

Most accounting tools track inventory as a dollar value, not as physical units. If you sell 100 SKUs across multiple channels, you need a tool that syncs inventory counts in real time—or at least daily. QuickBooks Online’s native inventory tracking is basic. Xero’s is better, but you’ll still need a separate app like DEAR Inventory or Cin7 if you’re managing complex stock levels.

### Multi-currency and tax compliance

If you sell internationally, you need a tool that handles foreign currency transactions and local tax rules. QuickBooks and Xero both support multi-currency, but Xero’s reporting is cleaner for VAT and GST. QuickBooks has better support for US sales tax, but its international tax settings are clunky.

## The rough edges

### QuickBooks Online

The biggest complaint I hear about QuickBooks is its transaction limits. The $30/month plan only allows 250 transactions per month. If you sync orders, bank feeds, and bills, you’ll hit that limit fast. Upgrading to the $80/month plan removes the limit, but that’s a big jump for small stores.

The mobile app is also weak. It’s fine for invoicing, but you can’t reconcile payouts or run reports on the go. If you’re managing your store from your phone, you’ll be frustrated.

### Xero

Xero’s biggest limitation is its lack of native ecommerce integrations. You’ll need A2X or another app to sync orders properly, and those apps add complexity. A2X is reliable, but it’s another tool to manage—and another cost.

Xero’s reporting is powerful, but it’s not intuitive. The dashboard gives you a high-level view, but digging into sales by product or channel takes multiple clicks. If you’re used to QuickBooks’ more straightforward reports, Xero will feel overwhelming at first.

### Wave

Wave is free, but its ecommerce integrations are limited. You can connect Shopify, but the sync is basic—it doesn’t handle refunds or fees well. If you’re on WooCommerce or another platform, you’ll need to manually enter orders.

Wave’s customer support is also slow. If you run into issues, you’re mostly on your own. For a free tool, that’s understandable, but it’s a trade-off.

## What users complain about

G2 reviews as of June 2026 show consistent pain points:

- **Xero**: Steep learning curve, weak mobile app, and reliance on third-party apps for ecommerce.
- **Wave**: Limited integrations, manual data entry, and slow support.

Reddit threads about ecommerce accounting often mention the same issues. ## Who should use what

### QuickBooks Online

Best for: US-based stores with simple inventory needs and under 500 orders/month.

QuickBooks is the most popular choice, and for good reason. It’s easy to set up, and most bookkeepers know it. If you’re just starting out and don’t need advanced inventory tracking, it’s a safe bet. Just watch those transaction limits.

### Xero

Best for: International stores, stores with complex inventory, or teams that need strong reporting.

Xero is more flexible than QuickBooks, but it’s not as user-friendly. If you sell in multiple currencies or need detailed inventory tracking, Xero is worth the extra effort. You’ll need A2X or another app for ecommerce, but the combination works well.

### Zoho Books

Best for: Budget-conscious stores that need basic ecommerce features.

Zoho Books is cheaper than QuickBooks and Xero, and it includes inventory tracking and multi-currency support. It’s not as polished as the other options, but it’s a good middle ground if you’re on a tight budget.

### Wave

Best for: Very small stores with simple needs and no budget for accounting software.

Wave is free, but it’s not a long-term solution. If you’re just starting out and only processing a few orders a month, it’s fine. Once you hit 100+ orders, you’ll outgrow it.

## What to watch for

QuickBooks is testing a new ecommerce integration that syncs orders and payouts without A2X. It’s in beta now, and if it works well, it could make QuickBooks a better fit for stores with high order volumes. Xero is also updating its inventory tracking, but the changes are minor—don’t expect a major overhaul soon.

If you’re on WooCommerce, check out WooCommerce Bookings. It’s not a full accounting tool, but it syncs orders to QuickBooks or Xero and handles refunds better than most native integrations.

## Final recommendation

If you’re a US-based store with under 500 orders a month, start with QuickBooks Online. It’s the easiest to set up, and most bookkeepers know it. If you’re selling internationally or need advanced inventory tracking, go with Xero and A2X. If you’re on a tight budget, Zoho Books is a good alternative.

Don’t overlook the migration effort. Moving from spreadsheets or another tool takes time, and you’ll likely need a bookkeeper to help. Factor that into your decision—sometimes paying more for a tool that’s easier to set up saves money in the long run.
