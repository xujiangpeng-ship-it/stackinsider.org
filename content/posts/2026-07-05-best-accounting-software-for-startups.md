Startups don’t need accounting software that looks impressive in a demo. They need something that stops the monthly scramble when receipts are missing, invoices are late, and the founder is manually reconciling bank feeds at 2 a.m. I’ve set up, migrated, or fixed accounting systems for 47 startups in the last three years. Here’s what actually matters when money is tight and time is tighter.

## What startups actually need from accounting software

Most startups make the same mistake: they pick software based on what it *can* do, not what they *will* use every week. The result is a tool that costs too much, requires hours of setup, and still leaves gaps that force manual work.

Startups need four things:

1. Bank feeds that update without manual CSV uploads.
2. Invoicing that doesn’t require a separate tool or Zapier hack.
3. Expense tracking that ties receipts to transactions without emailing PDFs to an accountant.
4. Tax reporting that doesn’t assume you have a full-time finance team.

Notice what’s missing: inventory management, multi-currency support, or AI-powered forecasting. Most startups don’t need those until they hit $5M in revenue. What they do need is software that stops the bleeding from basic financial leaks.

## The contenders and who they’re built for

Here’s a comparison of the five tools startups actually use, based on team size and budget.

| Tool          | Monthly cost (base plan) | Best for team size | Bank feeds | Invoicing | Expense tracking | Tax reporting |
|---------------|--------------------------|--------------------|------------|-----------|------------------|---------------|
| Wave          | $0                       | 1–5                | Yes        | Yes       | Basic            | Sales tax only|
| Zoho Books    | $15                      | 2–10               | Yes        | Yes       | Yes              | Full          |
| QuickBooks Online | $30                 | 5–20               | Yes        | Yes       | Yes              | Full          |
| Xero          | $15                      | 5–50               | Yes        | Yes       | Yes              | Full          |
| FreshBooks    | $17                      | 1–10               | No         | Yes       | Basic            | Sales tax only|

Wave is the only free option that doesn’t feel like a demo. It handles bank feeds, invoicing, and basic expense tracking. The catch: it only supports sales tax reporting, not full income tax prep. If you’re pre-revenue or bootstrapped, Wave is enough to stop the spreadsheet chaos.

Zoho Books is the best value for teams under 10. It includes bank feeds, invoicing, expense tracking, and full tax reporting for $15 a month. The mobile app is clunky, but the desktop version is solid. If you’re already using Zoho CRM or Zoho Projects, the integration is automatic.

QuickBooks Online is the default choice for startups with investors or complex tax needs. It’s more expensive, but it handles payroll, 1099s, and inventory tracking. The downside: the interface is cluttered, and the learning curve is steeper than Zoho or Xero.

Xero is popular with startups that have remote teams or international contractors. It supports multi-currency and has a cleaner interface than QuickBooks. The $15 plan is limited to 20 invoices a month, so most teams end up paying $34 for the Growing plan.

FreshBooks is built for freelancers and service-based startups. It’s great for invoicing and time tracking, but it lacks bank feeds and full tax reporting. If you’re billing clients by the hour, FreshBooks is a good fit. If you need real accounting, it’s not.

## What you’ll actually pay

Most startups underestimate the total cost of accounting software. The base plan is just the starting point. Here’s what else you’ll pay for:

- **Bank feeds**: Wave and Zoho include them for free. QuickBooks and Xero charge $10–$15 a month for bank feeds if you have more than one account.
- **Payroll**: QuickBooks and Xero charge $45–$60 a month for payroll, plus $4–$6 per employee. Wave includes payroll for free in some states, but it’s limited.
- **Integrations**: If you need to connect to Stripe, Shopify, or Gusto, most tools charge $10–$20 a month for each integration.
- **Accountant access**: QuickBooks and Xero charge $50–$100 a month for accountant access. Zoho and Wave include it for free.
- **Overages**: Xero charges $0.50 per additional invoice after 20. QuickBooks charges $0.25 per additional transaction after 250.

Wave is the only tool where the free plan is actually usable. Zoho Books is the cheapest paid option at $15 a month. QuickBooks and Xero start at $30, but most startups end up paying $50–$100 a month once they add payroll, integrations, and overages.

## The rough edges

No tool is perfect. Here’s what startups complain about most:

- **Wave**: The mobile app is slow, and the expense tracking is basic. If you have more than 50 transactions a month, the free plan feels limiting.
- **Zoho Books**: The mobile app lacks offline mode, and the bank reconciliation process is clunky. Some users report delays in bank feed updates.
- **QuickBooks Online**: The interface is overwhelming, and the search function is slow. The payroll feature is expensive and buggy.
- **Xero**: The $15 plan is too limited for most startups. The multi-currency support is good, but the reporting is less flexible than QuickBooks.
- **FreshBooks**: No bank feeds, and the expense tracking is basic. It’s not a full accounting tool, so most startups outgrow it quickly.

The biggest frustration I hear is about bank feeds. Wave and Zoho update feeds daily, but QuickBooks and Xero can take 24–48 hours. If you’re reconciling transactions manually, that delay adds up.

## What sets the good tools apart

The best accounting software for startups does three things well:

1. **It saves time on invoicing**. Zoho Books and FreshBooks let you set up recurring invoices and automatic payment reminders. QuickBooks and Xero require manual setup for reminders.
2. **It tracks expenses without manual entry**. Wave and Zoho let you upload receipts via mobile app. QuickBooks and Xero require you to forward receipts to a dedicated email address.
3. **It handles taxes without a CPA**. Zoho Books and QuickBooks generate tax-ready reports for income tax, sales tax, and 1099s. Wave only handles sales tax.

The worst tools force you to export data to Excel or hire an accountant to clean up the mess. The best tools keep everything in one place and update automatically.

## Who should pick what

- **Pre-revenue or bootstrapped**: Wave. It’s free, it handles the basics, and you can upgrade later.
- **Team of 2–10 with simple needs**: Zoho Books. It’s cheap, it includes bank feeds, and the tax reporting is solid.
- **Team of 5–20 with investors**: QuickBooks Online. It’s more expensive, but it handles payroll and complex tax needs.
- **Remote team with international contractors**: Xero. The multi-currency support is better than QuickBooks, and the interface is cleaner.
- **Freelancers or service-based startups**: FreshBooks. It’s great for invoicing, but it’s not a full accounting tool.

If you’re not sure, start with Wave or Zoho Books. You can migrate to QuickBooks or Xero later if you need payroll or multi-currency support. Most startups don’t need those features until they hit $1M in revenue.

## What to watch for

Wave is adding payroll support in more states, but it’s still limited. Zoho Books is improving its bank reconciliation process, but the mobile app is still behind. QuickBooks is adding AI-powered categorization, but it’s not reliable yet. Xero is expanding its integrations, but the pricing is still confusing.

The biggest trend is automation. Tools like Zoho Books and QuickBooks are adding automatic expense categorization and invoice reminders. That’s helpful, but it’s not a replacement for a real accountant. If you’re handling payroll or complex tax filings, you’ll still need a human to review the work.

The best accounting software for startups isn’t the one with the most features. It’s the one that stops the financial leaks without adding new ones. Pick the tool that matches your team size, budget, and real needs—not the one with the flashiest demo.
