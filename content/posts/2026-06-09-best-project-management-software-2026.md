ClickUp’s “Unlimited” plan isn’t unlimited—it caps automations at 1,000 per month. For a team of 10, that’s roughly 3 automations per person per day before you hit the wall. Most users don’t realize this until they’re mid-migration, and by then, the contract’s already signed.

## What Sets It Apart

The tools that still stand out in 2026 do one thing well: they don’t force teams into a single methodology. Asana, for example, finally added native time-blocking in Q1 2026, letting users drag tasks directly onto a calendar view without third-party integrations. This matters because hybrid teams—those mixing Agile sprints with waterfall deadlines—no longer need to juggle two separate tools. The feature isn’t flashy, but it eliminates the daily friction of switching between a task list and a calendar app.

Monday.com’s “Workdocs” (released in late 2025) is another quiet win. It’s not just another collaborative doc—it’s a living workspace where tasks, files, and comments coexist in the same view. For client-facing teams, this means no more exporting project updates to Google Docs or Notion just to share a polished report. The catch? Workdocs are only available on the Pro plan and above, which starts at $19/user/month.

## The Rough Edges

Notion’s project management layer is still a Frankenstein. The tool excels at documentation and wikis, but its task dependencies are clunky. Users report that setting up a simple “Task B starts when Task A finishes” requires manual date adjustments, unlike Asana or ClickUp, where dependencies auto-update. This isn’t a dealbreaker for small teams, but for agencies managing 50+ concurrent projects, it’s a time sink.

Jira’s pricing model is another sore spot. Atlassian’s “per user” pricing for Jira Software Cloud jumps from $7.75/user/month (Standard) to $15.25/user/month (Premium), but the Premium tier is the first to include audit logs and advanced roadmaps. For compliance-heavy industries like healthcare or finance, this isn’t optional—it’s a mandate. Teams often realize this too late, after setting up workflows in the Standard tier.

## What You’ll Actually Pay

Here’s how the top tools compare for a 20-person team in 2026, including the “gotchas” that don’t appear on pricing pages:

| Tool          | Plan          | Monthly Cost (20 users) | Hidden Costs / Limitations                     |
|---------------|---------------|-------------------------|-----------------------------------------------|
| Asana         | Advanced      | $1,200                  | No time-blocking on lower tiers               |
| ClickUp       | Business      | $2,000                  | 10,000 automations/month (not “unlimited”)    |
| Monday.com    | Pro           | $3,800                  | Workdocs only on Pro+                         |
| Jira          | Premium       | $3,050                  | Audit logs locked behind Premium              |
| Notion        | Business      | $1,600                  | No native dependencies; requires workarounds  |

*Note: Pricing reflects annual billing discounts and may vary by region.*

The most common sticker shock comes from integrations. Tools like ClickUp and Monday.com advertise “1,000+ integrations,” but many require third-party middleware (like Zapier or Make) to function. For example, Monday.com’s native Salesforce integration is read-only unless you’re on the Enterprise plan. Teams often assume integrations are plug-and-play, only to discover they need a developer to set up API calls.

## Where It Falls Short

Smartsheet’s mobile app is a mess. The interface is a desktop experience crammed into a phone screen, and offline mode is nonexistent. For field teams or remote workers, this is a dealbreaker. The company’s Q4 2025 roadmap promised a redesign, but as of June 2026, it’s still in beta.

Another underreported issue: migration effort. Teams switching from Trello to ClickUp or Asana often underestimate the time required to rebuild workflows. Trello’s power-ups (like Butler for automations) don’t translate cleanly, and users report spending 2-3 weeks reconfiguring rules in the new tool. Vendors downplay this in their “easy migration” marketing, but community forums like Reddit’s r/projectmanagement are full of war stories.

## Who Should (and Shouldn’t) Use These Tools

**Asana** is the best fit for teams that need flexibility without complexity. If your workflows mix Agile, Kanban, and waterfall, Asana’s hybrid views (timeline, board, list) keep everyone aligned. It’s also the only tool in this list with a free plan that doesn’t feel like a demo (up to 15 users, basic features).

**ClickUp** is for power users who want to customize everything. The trade-off? It’s overwhelming for small teams. If you’re a 5-person startup, ClickUp’s 100+ features will slow you down. But for agencies or product teams managing multiple projects, the custom fields and nested subtasks are worth the learning curve.

**Monday.com** shines for client-facing teams. The Workdocs feature and polished dashboards make it easy to share updates with stakeholders. However, the pricing escalates quickly—once you hit 20 users, you’re locked into the Pro plan, which is nearly double the cost of Asana’s Advanced tier.

**Jira** is still the king of software development, but it’s overkill for non-technical teams. The terminology (epics, sprints, issues) confuses marketers and HR teams, and the setup requires a dedicated admin. If you’re not building software, look elsewhere.

**Notion** is ideal for teams that prioritize documentation over task management. It’s not a true project management tool, but if your workflow revolves around wikis, meeting notes, and lightweight tasks, Notion’s all-in-one workspace is hard to beat. Just don’t expect robust dependencies or reporting.

If you’re a remote team with field workers, avoid Smartsheet and Jira. The mobile apps are afterthoughts, and offline functionality is either limited or nonexistent. Asana or ClickUp are better bets for teams that need to update tasks on the go.

For teams on a tight budget, Asana’s free plan is the only one that doesn’t feel like a trial. ClickUp’s free tier is generous (unlimited users, 100MB file uploads), but the lack of time tracking and advanced reporting makes it a non-starter for professional use. Monday.com’s free plan is a teaser—it caps at 2 users and 1,000 items, which is useless for even the smallest teams.
