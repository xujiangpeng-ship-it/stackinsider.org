Zoho Projects and Wrike both promise to organize your work, but their pricing models push teams in opposite directions. Zoho starts cheap and scales with user count. Wrike starts expensive and scales with features. If you’re a 10-person agency, you’ll pay $120 a month for Zoho’s Premium plan. The same team on Wrike’s Professional tier jumps to $240. That gap widens as you add users or need automation.

I’ve migrated teams from spreadsheets to both tools. The sticker price is only half the story. Zoho’s free tier locks basic Gantt charts behind a paywall. Wrike’s free plan is essentially a read-only board. Neither vendor advertises the extra cost of SSO or advanced reporting until you’re already committed.

## What you’ll actually pay

Zoho Projects pricing (as of June 2026):
- Free: up to 3 users, 2 projects, 10 MB storage.
- Premium: $5/user/month (billed annually), 50 projects, 100 GB storage.
- Enterprise: $10/user/month (billed annually), unlimited projects, 120 GB storage.

Wrike pricing (as of June 2026):
- Free: up to 5 users, basic task views, 2 GB storage.
- Professional: $9.80/user/month (billed annually), Gantt charts, 2 GB storage.
- Business: $24.80/user/month (billed annually), custom workflows, 5 GB storage.
- Enterprise: custom quote, SSO, 10 GB storage.

Zoho’s Premium plan includes time tracking and automation. Wrike charges an extra $5/user/month for time tracking on the Business tier. If you need SSO, Wrike forces you into the Enterprise plan, which starts at $35/user/month. Zoho offers SSO on the Enterprise tier for $10/user/month.

Storage is the quiet budget killer. Zoho gives 100 GB on Premium. Wrike’s Business plan caps at 5 GB. Teams that attach files to tasks will hit that limit fast. Wrike’s storage add-on costs $10/month for 100 GB. Zoho’s add-on is $5/month for the same amount.

## Where the pricing models hurt

Zoho’s per-user pricing feels fair until you hit 20 users. At that point, the $100/month jump to Enterprise is steep if you only need SSO. Wrike’s tiered feature gates force upgrades before teams are ready. A 15-person team might only need custom workflows, but Wrike’s Business plan is the first tier that offers them. That’s a $372/month commitment.

Both tools nickel-and-dime integrations. Zoho’s native integrations with Zoho CRM and Zoho Books are seamless, but third-party apps like Slack or Google Drive require Zapier. Wrike’s native integrations cover more ground, but advanced API access is locked behind the Enterprise plan. If you’re a HubSpot shop, Wrike’s native integration is a lifesaver. Zoho’s HubSpot integration is clunky and often breaks after updates.

## What teams actually get for the money

Zoho Projects shines for small teams that need Gantt charts and time tracking without fuss. The mobile app is functional, though offline mode is missing. Wrike’s interface is cleaner, but the learning curve is steeper. New users struggle with Wrike’s folder hierarchy and custom item types. Zoho’s simpler structure means less training.

Automation is where Wrike pulls ahead. Zoho’s automation builder is basic. You can set up triggers like “when status changes to ‘In Review,’ notify the project lead,” but complex multi-step workflows require workarounds. Wrike’s automation engine handles branching logic and conditional actions natively. For teams that rely on automated reminders or approval chains, Wrike’s Business plan is worth the premium.

Reporting is the opposite. Zoho’s built-in reports cover 80% of what most teams need. Wrike’s reporting is powerful but overwhelming. The default dashboards are cluttered, and custom reports require SQL-like queries. Zoho’s reports are exportable to Excel with one click. Wrike’s exports are PDF-only unless you upgrade to Enterprise.

## The rough edges

Zoho’s support is slow. Response times average 24 hours, and live chat is only available on the Enterprise plan. Wrike’s support is faster but inconsistent. Some users report getting helpful answers within hours. Others wait days for a resolution. Both tools have active user communities, but Zoho’s forums are harder to navigate.

Wrike’s mobile app is buggy. Push notifications fail to sync, and the offline mode is unreliable. Zoho’s mobile app is stable but lacks features like task dependencies. Neither app supports two-factor authentication via SMS. You’ll need an authenticator app for both.

## Who should pick which

Pick Zoho Projects if:
- You’re a team of 5-20 people.
- You need time tracking and Gantt charts without extra fees.
- Your budget is tight, and you can live with slower support.

Pick Wrike if:
- You’re a team of 15-100 people.
- You need advanced automation and custom workflows.
- You’re already using HubSpot, Salesforce, or Adobe Creative Cloud.

If you’re a nonprofit, Zoho offers a 20% discount. Wrike’s nonprofit discount is 15%. Neither discount applies to add-ons like storage or time tracking.

Both tools offer free trials. Zoho’s trial is 10 days. Wrike’s is 14 days. Use the trial to test storage limits and automation. Most teams hit a wall with one of those before the trial ends.

Wrike’s next pricing update is rumored for Q4 2026. The Business plan may drop to $22/user/month, but storage limits will likely stay the same. Zoho hasn’t announced any changes, but their last update in 2025 added 20 GB to the Premium plan storage. Watch for storage adjustments in both tools.
