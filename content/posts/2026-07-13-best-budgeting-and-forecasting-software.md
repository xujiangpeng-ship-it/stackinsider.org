Most budgeting software looks good in a demo. Then you try to connect it to your actual general ledger, and the cracks show. I’ve helped teams migrate from spreadsheets to dedicated tools, and the difference between “works in theory” and “works on Tuesday” comes down to a few specific things: how it handles actuals, whether the forecast model matches your business rhythm, and whether the CFO will actually use it without calling IT for help.

Here’s what matters when you’re picking software that won’t just sit on the shelf.

## What you’ll actually pay

Pricing for budgeting tools isn’t just about the monthly fee. The real cost shows up in implementation, training, and the time your team spends fighting the tool instead of using it.

| Tool            | Starting price (annual) | User minimum | Hidden costs to watch for                     |
|-----------------|-------------------------|--------------|-----------------------------------------------|
| Adaptive Insights | $25,000                | 10           | Implementation often doubles the first-year cost. |
| Planful         | $30,000                | 15           | Additional modules for workforce planning add $10k+. |
| Vena            | $18,000                | 5            | Excel-based workflows can slow down larger teams. |
| Jirav           | $12,000                | 3            | QuickBooks integration is solid; NetSuite requires custom work. |
| Cube            | $15,000                | 5            | No native consolidation for multi-entity setups. |

Adaptive Insights and Planful are enterprise plays. If you’re under 50 employees, the implementation cost alone can eat your entire annual budget. Vena and Cube work well for teams that still rely on Excel but want version control and audit trails. Jirav is the only one on this list that doesn’t charge extra for API access, which matters if you’re pulling data from multiple sources.

## Features that actually matter

### Forecasting that matches your business

Most tools let you build a forecast. Few let you build one that reflects how your business actually works.

Adaptive Insights has a flexible modeling engine that can handle complex revenue recognition rules. If you’re in SaaS with deferred revenue, this is the only tool I’ve seen that doesn’t force you into a workaround. The trade-off: the learning curve is steep. Your FP&A team will need training, and finance users outside that group will struggle with the interface.

Jirav, on the other hand, is built for small teams that need a forecast fast. It uses a driver-based model that ties headcount and revenue directly to expenses. This works well for service businesses but falls short for inventory-heavy companies. The forecast updates automatically when you change assumptions, which is great until you realize you can’t override it without breaking the model.

### Actuals that don’t require a data team

Connecting to your general ledger should be simple. In practice, it’s often a mess.

Vena and Cube both use Excel as the front end, which means your team already knows how to use them. The catch: Excel’s limitations become the tool’s limitations. Vena has a better audit trail and version control, but Cube’s integration with Google Sheets is smoother if your team lives in Drive.

Planful and Adaptive Insights have native connectors for NetSuite, Intacct, and SAP. The problem isn’t the connection—it’s the mapping. If your chart of accounts doesn’t match the tool’s default structure, you’ll spend weeks setting up custom dimensions. I’ve seen teams give up and export to CSV instead, which defeats the purpose of automation.

### Reporting that finance and operations can both use

Most tools generate reports. Few generate reports that non-finance teams will actually read.

Jirav’s dashboards are the most accessible. They’re visual, interactive, and update in real time. The downside: they’re not customizable enough for complex board presentations. You’ll still need to export to PowerPoint and manually tweak the formatting.

Adaptive Insights has the opposite problem. The reporting is powerful but requires SQL-like syntax for anything beyond basic views. If your FP&A team is comfortable with that, great. If not, you’ll be stuck with canned reports that don’t answer the questions your CEO is asking.

## The rough edges

### Adaptive Insights
The modeling engine is powerful but slow. Large models can take minutes to recalculate, which makes iterative forecasting painful. The mobile app is read-only, so don’t expect to approve budgets from your phone.

### Planful
The interface feels dated. The workflow for submitting and approving budgets is clunky, and the audit trail isn’t as detailed as Adaptive’s. Workforce planning is a separate module with a separate cost.

### Vena
Excel-based tools sound familiar, but they don’t scale. Vena’s version control works well for small teams, but larger organizations hit performance limits when multiple users are in the same file.

### Jirav
The forecast model is rigid. If your business doesn’t fit the driver-based approach, you’ll spend more time fighting the tool than using it. The QuickBooks integration is solid, but NetSuite users report sync issues.

### Cube
No native consolidation for multi-entity setups. If you have multiple legal entities, you’ll need to export and combine data manually. The Google Sheets integration is a plus, but the lack of a proper API is a dealbreaker for some teams.

## What users complain about

G2 reviews as of June 2026 show a few consistent pain points:

- **Adaptive Insights**: 22% of negative reviews mention the steep learning curve. One user said, “We hired a consultant to set it up, and now we need them for every minor change.”
- **Planful**: 18% of negative reviews cite poor customer support. Another common complaint: “The workforce planning module is overpriced for what it does.”
- **Vena**: 15% of negative reviews mention performance issues with large Excel files. “It’s like Excel on steroids, but the steroids slow it down,” one reviewer wrote.
- **Jirav**: 12% of negative reviews say the forecast model is too rigid. “If your business doesn’t fit their template, you’re out of luck,” another noted.
- **Cube**: 10% of negative reviews mention the lack of multi-entity support. “We outgrew it in six months,” one user said.

## Who each tool is for

- **Adaptive Insights**: Large enterprises with complex revenue recognition rules and a dedicated FP&A team. If you’re not willing to invest in training, look elsewhere.
- **Planful**: Mid-market companies that need workforce planning and can afford the implementation cost. If you’re not using the workforce module, you’re overpaying.
- **Vena**: Teams that still use Excel but want better version control and audit trails. If you’re over 100 employees, the performance issues will frustrate you.
- **Jirav**: Small service businesses that need a forecast fast and don’t have complex inventory or multi-entity needs. The QuickBooks integration is a plus.
- **Cube**: Startups and small teams that want a lightweight tool with Google Sheets integration. If you need multi-entity support, this isn’t it.

If you’re a 50-person SaaS company with deferred revenue, Adaptive Insights is the only tool on this list that won’t force you into workarounds. The implementation cost is high, but the alternative is a spreadsheet that breaks every time you update it.

If you’re a 20-person consulting firm using QuickBooks, Jirav will save you time. The forecast model is simple, but that’s a feature, not a bug, for teams that don’t need complexity.

If you’re somewhere in between, Vena or Cube might work, but only if you’re okay with Excel’s limitations. The trade-off is familiarity—your team already knows how to use them, which means less training and faster adoption.

The best budgeting and forecasting software isn’t the one with the most features. It’s the one that matches how your business actually works and doesn’t make your team hate their jobs. Pick that one.
