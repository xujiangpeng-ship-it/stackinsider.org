## Features That Actually Matter (and Where They Fall Short)

### The Good: What Free Plans Do Well
**Trello’s board system** is still the easiest way to visualize simple workflows. For small teams or personal projects, the drag-and-drop interface is intuitive enough that even non-technical users adopt it without training. The free plan’s unlimited boards mean you can spin up a new project in seconds—no approvals, no setup fees.

**Asana’s timeline view** (available in the free tier) is a rare exception to the "paywall advanced features" rule. If you’re managing a one-off project with dependencies, this alone might justify choosing Asana over Trello or ClickUp. Just don’t expect to use it with more than 15 teammates.

### The Bad: What You’ll Miss
**ClickUp’s free plan lacks Gantt charts**—a dealbreaker for teams that rely on visual timelines. Their workaround? A "List" view with start/end dates, which isn’t the same as a true Gantt. Users in the ClickUp subreddit report this forces them to upgrade to the $7/user/month plan just to get basic dependency tracking.

**Notion’s free plan caps file uploads at 5MB**. If your team collaborates on documents, spreadsheets, or images, this limit becomes frustrating fast. One user on Indie Hackers noted they had to switch to Google Drive for file storage, which defeated the purpose of an all-in-one tool.

**Automation is a tease**. Trello’s 50-command monthly limit sounds generous until you realize a single recurring task (e.g., "Move overdue cards to ‘Blocked�?every Monday") eats up 4 commands *per execution*. ClickUp’s free plan has no automation at all.

## The Rough Edges: What Users Complain About

**Migration headaches** are a recurring theme. Teams that start on Trello’s free plan often hit a wall when they need to export data for a paid tool. Trello’s export feature (available in free plans) only outputs JSON or CSV—useless for migrating to tools like Asana or ClickUp without manual reformatting. One user on Stack Overflow documented spending 12 hours manually recreating a 200-card board after realizing the export was incompatible.

**Mobile apps are an afterthought**. ClickUp’s mobile app, for example, doesn’t support offline mode—a problem for field teams or anyone with spotty Wi-Fi. Notion’s mobile app is notoriously slow, with users reporting lag when loading databases with more than 50 entries. Trello’s app is the most polished, but it lacks the ability to create or edit automation rules, forcing users to switch to desktop.

**Support is nonexistent**. Free plans come with community support only. ClickUp’s free users report waiting 3�? days for responses in their help center, while Trello’s community forums are filled with unanswered questions. Asana’s free plan includes no support at all—just a knowledge base.

## Where Free Plans Make Sense (and Where They Don’t)

**Good for:**
- Solo founders or freelancers managing a handful of clients.
- Small teams (under 10 people) with simple workflows (e.g., content calendars, basic task tracking).
- Nonprofits or volunteer groups with no budget for software.

**Avoid if:**
- You need Gantt charts, dependencies, or advanced reporting.
- Your team collaborates on large files (designs, videos, datasets).
- You rely on automation to reduce manual work.
- You’re a growing team that might need to scale quickly—migrating later is painful.
