### Asana: the safe default

Asana works for agencies that want something simple and don’t need deep file management. The UI is clean, and the timeline view is useful for mapping out deliverables. But it treats files as attachments, not assets. If your team lives in Google Drive or Dropbox, this isn’t a dealbreaker. If you need to preview PSDs or Figma files without downloading them, it’s frustrating.

One hidden cost: Asana’s free plan is generous, but agencies quickly hit the 15-person limit. The jump to the Premium tier ($10.99/user/month) is worth it for the timeline and forms, but the Business tier ($24.99/user/month) adds features most agencies don’t need, like workload tracking.

### ClickUp: the Swiss Army knife

ClickUp is the most flexible tool on this list. You can build custom workflows, track time, manage assets, and even host docs. For agencies that want one tool to rule them all, it’s tempting.

The problem is complexity. ClickUp’s strength is also its weakness. I’ve seen teams waste weeks configuring it, only to abandon it when they realize no one wants to learn a new system. If you’re willing to invest in setup and training, it’s powerful. If you need something that works out of the box, it’s overkill.

### Monday.com: the visual choice

Monday.com’s strength is its visual appeal. The color-coded boards make it easy to see project status at a glance. For agencies that need to impress clients with dashboards, it’s a good fit.

But Monday.com is expensive for what it offers. The Basic plan ($8/user/month) lacks time tracking and automation. The Standard plan ($10/user/month) adds those features but still feels limited. The Pro plan ($16/user/month) is where it becomes useful, but by then, you’re paying more than Asana or ClickUp for similar functionality.

### Trello: the simple option

Trello is the only tool on this list that’s truly simple. The card-based system is easy to understand, and the free plan is enough for small teams. For agencies with straightforward workflows, it’s a good choice.

But Trello lacks depth. There’s no built-in time tracking, and the Power-Ups (add-ons) feel tacked on. If you need to manage complex projects with multiple stakeholders, it’s not enough.

### Airtable: the database hybrid

Airtable is a spreadsheet-database hybrid, not a true PM tool. But for agencies that need to track assets, clients, and projects in one place, it’s useful. The ability to create custom views (Kanban, calendar, gallery) is powerful.

The downside is that Airtable isn’t built for project management. There’s no built-in time tracking, and the automation features are limited. If you’re already using it for asset management, it might work as a PM tool. Otherwise, it’s better as a complement.

### Wrike: the proofing specialist

Wrike is the only tool on this list with built-in proofing tools. For agencies that need to collect feedback on designs, videos, or documents, it’s a strong choice. The approval workflows are well-designed, and the asset management is better than most.

But Wrike is expensive. The Professional plan ($9.80/user/month) lacks time tracking and custom fields. The Business plan ($24.80/user/month) adds those features but is overkill for most agencies. The UI also feels outdated compared to Asana or ClickUp.

### Basecamp: the flat-rate option

Basecamp is the only tool on this list with a flat-rate pricing model. For $15/user/month (or $299/month for unlimited users), you get all features. For agencies with fluctuating team sizes, this is a big advantage.

But Basecamp feels outdated. The UI hasn’t changed in years, and the features are basic. There’s no time tracking, and the asset management is weak. For agencies that need a simple tool and don’t want to worry about per-user costs, it’s a good fit. For everyone else, it’s limiting.

## What sets the good tools apart

The tools that work for creative agencies share a few traits:

1. **Client-friendly collaboration.** Asana, Wrike, and Monday.com all let you invite clients as guests. This is non-negotiable. If a tool forces clients to create an account or navigate a complex UI, it’s a no-go.
2. **Asset previews.** Wrike and ClickUp let you preview files without downloading them. This saves time and reduces errors. Asana and Trello don’t.
3. **Flexible views.** Agencies need to switch between Kanban, timeline, and calendar views. ClickUp and Airtable do this well. Trello and Basecamp don’t.
4. **Time tracking.** ClickUp, Monday.com, and Wrike all have built-in time tracking. Asana and Trello require integrations.

The best tool for your agency depends on your workflow. If you need proofing tools, Wrike is the only real option. If you want something simple, Asana or Trello work. If you need flexibility, ClickUp is the best choice.

## The rough edges

No tool is perfect. Here’s what users complain about:

- **Asana:** The mobile app is slow. The search function is weak. There’s no offline mode.
- **ClickUp:** The learning curve is steep. The UI can feel cluttered. Some features are buggy.
- **Monday.com:** The pricing is confusing. The automation features are limited. The mobile app lacks key features.
- **Trello:** The free plan is too limited. The paid plans are expensive for what you get. There’s no built-in time tracking.
- **Airtable:** It’s not a true PM tool. The automation features are weak. The UI can feel overwhelming.
- **Wrike:** The UI is outdated. The pricing is high. The mobile app is slow.
- **Basecamp:** The UI feels old. The features are basic. There’s no time tracking.

## What you’ll actually pay

Pricing is where most tools get sneaky. Here’s what to expect:

- **Asana:** The free plan is enough for small teams, but the jump to Premium ($10.99/user/month) is worth it. The Business plan ($24.99/user/month) adds features most agencies don’t need.
- **ClickUp:** The free plan is generous, but the Unlimited plan ($7/user/month) is where it becomes useful. The Business plan ($12/user/month) adds time tracking and automation.
- **Monday.com:** The Basic plan ($8/user/month) is too limited. The Standard plan ($10/user/month) is the minimum viable option. The Pro plan ($16/user/month) is where it becomes powerful.
- **Trello:** The free plan is enough for small teams. The Standard plan ($5/user/month) adds automation. The Premium plan ($10/user/month) adds views and security.
- **Airtable:** The free plan is limited. The Plus plan ($10/user/month) adds sync and automation. The Pro plan ($20/user/month) adds advanced features.
- **Wrike:** The Professional plan ($9.80/user/month) is too limited. The Business plan ($24.80/user/month) is where it becomes useful.
- **Basecamp:** The flat-rate pricing ($15/user/month or $299/month for unlimited users) is a big advantage.

Hidden costs to watch for:
- **Integrations:** Some tools charge extra for integrations. ClickUp and Monday.com include most integrations in their paid plans. Asana and Wrike charge extra for some.
- **Training:** ClickUp and Monday.com require training. Asana and Trello don’t.
- **Storage:** Most tools include storage in their paid plans, but Wrike charges extra for additional storage.

## Who should pick what

- **Small agencies (1–10 people):** Trello or Asana. Both are simple and affordable. If you need proofing tools, Wrike is the only option.
- **Mid-sized agencies (10–50 people):** Asana or ClickUp. Asana is simpler. ClickUp is more flexible.
- **Large agencies (50+ people):** ClickUp or Monday.com. ClickUp is more customizable. Monday.com is more visual.
- **Agencies with complex workflows:** ClickUp. It’s the most flexible tool on this list.
- **Agencies that need proofing tools:** Wrike. It’s the only tool with built-in proofing.
- **Agencies on a tight budget:** Trello or Basecamp. Trello is cheaper for small teams. Basecamp is cheaper for large teams.

## The one thing no one tells you

Most agencies end up using two tools: one for project management and one for asset management. Asana + Google Drive. ClickUp + Dropbox. Wrike + Figma.

This isn’t ideal, but it’s reality. The tools that try to do everything (like ClickUp) end up doing nothing well. The tools that focus on one thing (like Asana) force you to integrate with other tools.

If you’re evaluating tools, ask your team: What’s the one thing we hate about our current setup? Then pick a tool that solves that problem. Don’t try to solve everything at once.

For most agencies, that one thing is client collaboration. Asana and Wrike do this well. ClickUp does it, but with more friction. Trello and Basecamp don’t.

If you’re already using a tool and it’s working, don’t switch. The grass isn’t greener. The tools that look shiny in demos are often the ones that cause the most headaches in real life.

Pick the tool that solves your biggest pain point, and accept that it won’t solve everything. That’s how agencies actually work.
