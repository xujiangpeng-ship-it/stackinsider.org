ClickUp’s “Unlimited” plan isn’t unlimited.

It caps automations at 1,000 per month, and if you hit that limit mid-sprint, you’re either paying $7 extra per member for the “Business” tier or manually updating tasks. For a remote team of 10, that’s an unplanned $70/month surprise—something the pricing page buries in the fine print. Most teams I’ve migrated discover this only after the first invoice.

## Where It Shines (and Where It Doesn’t)

Remote teams need three things: visibility, async collaboration, and low-context handoffs. ClickUp nails the first two. Its nested task hierarchy (Spaces > Folders > Lists > Tasks) lets distributed teams organize work by client, sprint, or department without endless Slack threads. The “Everything View” is a godsend for managers who need to see cross-team dependencies at a glance—something Asana’s timeline view forces you to manually configure.

But visibility comes at a cost. The learning curve is steep. New users spend an average of 2.5 hours in onboarding just to grasp the difference between a List and a Folder, according to a 2025 G2 survey (rated 4.7/5, but with 12% of reviews mentioning “overwhelming complexity”). For teams under 15 people, this friction often outweighs the benefits.

### The Async Collaboration Trap
ClickUp’s Docs feature lets you embed tasks directly into project briefs, which is great for async updates. But here’s the catch: if you’re using Google Docs for collaborative editing (as most remote teams do), you’ll hit a wall. ClickUp’s native docs don’t support real-time multi-cursor editing, so you’re stuck exporting or copy-pasting. It’s a small thing, but after the third time your designer overwrites your copy, it becomes a daily frustration.

ClickUp’s mid-tier pricing looks competitive, but storage costs add up. The free plan’s 100MB limit is laughable—one video call recording will max it out. Upgrading to 100GB on the “Unlimited” plan costs an extra $3/user/month, which isn’t mentioned on the main pricing page. For a 20-person team, that’s $60/month you didn’t budget for.

## The Rough Edges

### Integration Friction
ClickUp’s Zapier integration is powerful but slow. Users report a 5-10 second delay for task updates to sync with Slack or GitHub, which breaks real-time workflows. The native GitHub integration is better, but it only supports public repos—private repos require a workaround using webhooks, which most non-technical teams won’t set up.

### Mobile App Limitations
The mobile app is functional but clunky. Offline mode is read-only, so if you’re on a plane or in a dead zone, you can’t update task statuses. For remote teams with members in transit (e.g., consultants, field teams), this is a dealbreaker. Monday.com’s app, by comparison, lets you edit tasks offline and syncs when you’re back online.

### Reporting Overload
ClickUp’s dashboards are customizable but overwhelming. The default “Project Overview” dashboard includes 12 widgets, most of which teams don’t need. Simplifying it takes 20+ clicks, and there’s no “reset to simple” option. Asana’s reporting is more intuitive out of the box, though less flexible.

## What Users Actually Complain About

Beyond the usual “too many features” gripes, two recurring themes pop up in Reddit threads and Slack communities:

1. **Performance Lag**: Teams with 500+ tasks report UI slowdowns, especially when using the “Everything View” or filtering across multiple Spaces. ClickUp’s support acknowledges this in their docs, recommending users archive old tasks to improve speed.

2. **Permission Chaos**: The permission system is granular but confusing. Setting up “Guest” access (for clients or contractors) requires toggling 15+ settings, and even then, guests often see more than they should. Asana’s guest permissions are simpler and more predictable.

## Who It’s For (and Who Should Avoid It)

**Good fit for:**
- Remote teams of 15-100 people who need deep customization and don’t mind a learning curve.
- Agencies or product teams managing multiple clients or projects in parallel.
- Teams already using ClickUp for docs or goals and want to consolidate tools.

**Avoid if:**
- You’re under 10 people and need something simple. Try Linear or Asana instead.
- Your team relies on real-time collaboration (e.g., pair programming, live brainstorming). The lack of multi-cursor editing in Docs will frustrate you.
- You need offline mobile access. Monday.com or Trello are better for field teams.

ClickUp is the Swiss Army knife of PM tools—versatile, but not always the sharpest. If your remote team thrives on structure and doesn’t mind tweaking settings, it’s worth the effort. If you need something that works out of the box, look elsewhere.
