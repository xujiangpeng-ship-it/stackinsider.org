QuickBooks Online’s "Simple Start" plan caps users at **one billable employee**—not one user, but one *employee* whose payroll you can run. If you’re a five-person team with two owners and three staff, you’ll need to jump to the $85/month "Plus" plan just to pay everyone. Sage Accounting, meanwhile, includes payroll for up to 10 employees in its $25/month "Start" tier, but only if you’re in the U.S. or Canada. That’s the kind of fine print that turns a $20/month difference into a $600/year surprise.

## What You’ll Actually Pay

Here’s how the pricing shakes out for a 10-person SMB with basic inventory and payroll needs:

| Feature                     | QuickBooks Online Plus | Sage Accounting Start | Sage Accounting Pro |
|-----------------------------|------------------------|-----------------------|---------------------|
| Monthly cost (USD)          | $85                    | $25                   | $50                 |
| Max users                   | 5                      | 2                     | 10                  |
| Payroll included            | No (add $50 + $5/emp)  | Yes (10 emp)          | Yes (10 emp)        |
| Inventory tracking          | Yes                    | No                    | Yes                 |
| Bank reconciliation         | Yes                    | Yes                   | Yes                 |
| Mobile receipt capture      | Yes                    | Yes                   | Yes                 |
| Third-party app integrations| 750+                   | 30+                   | 30+                 |

QuickBooks’ pricing scales aggressively with add-ons. The "Advanced" plan ($200/month) is the only one that includes batch invoicing, but it also locks you into a 12-month contract—no month-to-month option. Sage’s pricing is flatter, but its app ecosystem is anemic. If you rely on niche tools like Shopify POS or a custom CRM, QuickBooks’ 750+ integrations are a lifeline. Sage’s 30+ integrations feel like an afterthought, and its API documentation hasn’t been updated since 2022 (verified via [Sage Developer Portal](https://developer.sage.com/accounting/) as of May 2026).

## Where It Shines (and Where It Doesn’t)

### QuickBooks: The Swiss Army Knife with a Learning Curve
QuickBooks’ biggest strength is its flexibility. The "Rules" feature for bank feeds lets you auto-categorize transactions based on keywords, payees, or amounts. For a retail business with 200+ daily transactions, this cuts reconciliation time from hours to minutes. The mobile app also syncs receipts in real time, which is a godsend for field teams. But that flexibility comes with complexity. The "Undeposited Funds" account, for example, is a common stumbling block—users frequently post payments directly to the bank account, creating duplicate entries. Intuit’s own support forums show **1,200+ threads** (as of June 2026) tagged with "undeposited funds" and "duplicate transactions."

### Sage: The Straightforward Choice with Hard Limits
Sage’s interface is cleaner, but its rigidity can be frustrating. The inventory module doesn’t support bundles or kits—if you sell a "Starter Pack" with three SKUs, you’ll need to manually adjust stock levels for each item. QuickBooks handles this natively. Sage also lacks a built-in time-tracking tool, forcing service-based businesses to use a third-party app (and pay extra). On the flip side, Sage’s reporting is more customizable out of the box. The "Financial Reporter" tool lets you drag-and-drop account codes into custom reports without exporting to Excel—a feature QuickBooks reserves for its $200/month plan.

## The Rough Edges

**QuickBooks’ hidden costs:** The "Live Bookkeeping" add-on ($500/month) is pitched as a way to offload accounting work, but it’s essentially a concierge service for the software you already pay for. Many users report being upsold to this service during support calls, even when their issue could be resolved with a five-minute fix.

**Sage’s migration headaches:** Sage’s data import tool only accepts CSV files in a specific format, and it doesn’t map custom fields. If you’re switching from QuickBooks, you’ll need to manually reformat your chart of accounts, customer list, and vendor data. The company’s migration guide (last updated in 2023) still references QuickBooks Desktop 2020, which hasn’t been supported since 2023. Reddit threads like r/bookkeeping are full of users who gave up and hired a consultant to handle the transition.

**Community sentiment:** On G2, QuickBooks Online has a **4.3/5 rating** (based on 12,000+ reviews as of June 2026), but the most common complaint is "feature bloat." Sage Accounting sits at **4.1/5** (3,000+ reviews), with users praising its simplicity but criticizing the lack of integrations. One reviewer put it bluntly: "Sage is like a flip phone—it makes calls, but don’t ask it to do anything else."

## Who Should Pick What

If you’re a **retail or e-commerce business** with complex inventory, QuickBooks is the only viable option. The integrations with Shopify, Square, and Amazon are seamless, and the batch invoicing feature (on the Advanced plan) saves hours for high-volume sellers. Just budget for the add-ons—payroll, time tracking, and advanced reporting will push your monthly cost to $150+.

If you’re a **service-based business** with straightforward accounting needs, Sage’s lower price and included payroll might be enough. But if you rely on third-party tools (like a niche CRM or project management software), you’ll hit a wall fast. Sage’s lack of API updates means even basic integrations can break without warning.

For **teams with remote workers or field staff**, QuickBooks’ mobile app is the clear winner. Sage’s app is functional but lacks offline mode, which is a dealbreaker for teams in areas with spotty service. QuickBooks also syncs receipts and expenses in real time, while Sage requires manual uploads.

The bottom line? QuickBooks is the more powerful tool, but it’s also the more expensive and complex one. Sage is simpler and cheaper, but its limitations will frustrate teams that need to scale. If you’re on the fence, start with QuickBooks’ 30-day free trial—just set a calendar reminder to cancel before the auto-renewal hits. Sage offers a 7-day trial, but you’ll need to enter payment details upfront. Neither company makes it easy to leave, so pick carefully.
