Most Amazon sellers start with spreadsheets. They end with spreadsheets too, but messier. The problem isn’t that accounting software doesn’t exist. It’s that most tools either ignore Amazon’s quirks or charge extra for features you assumed were standard.

I’ve set up accounting systems for over 40 Amazon brands, from solo sellers to 7-figure FBA businesses. Here’s what the marketing pages won’t tell you.

## What Amazon sellers actually need from accounting software

Amazon doesn’t send clean invoices. It sends settlement reports that lump together sales, refunds, storage fees, and advertising costs into a single CSV. Most accounting tools expect separate transactions. If the software can’t break these apart automatically, you’ll spend hours each month reconciling numbers that should match but don’t.

You also need:
- COGS tracking that syncs with inventory levels. Amazon’s FBA reports don’t include unit costs, so you’ll either enter them manually or connect to a separate inventory tool.
- Sales tax handling for multiple states. Amazon collects and remits tax in most states now, but you still need to file returns and track nexus.
- Currency conversion if you sell on international marketplaces. Amazon settles in your bank’s currency, but the reports are in USD, EUR, or JPY.
- Advertising cost allocation. Sponsored Products and DSP spend should flow into your P&L, not sit in a separate ad dashboard.

If the software doesn’t handle these, you’ll patch the gaps with spreadsheets. And spreadsheets break.

## The four tools Amazon sellers actually use

### 1. A2X
A2X sits between Amazon and your accounting software. It reads settlement reports, splits them into line items, and posts journal entries. You connect it once, then forget about it.

The good:
- Handles multiple marketplaces (US, CA, UK, DE, JP, etc.) in one account.
- Posts entries by product, so you can see profitability per SKU.
- Integrates with QuickBooks Online, Xero, and NetSuite.
- Reconciles automatically. If the numbers don’t match, it flags the discrepancy.

The bad:
- No direct bank feed. You’ll still need QuickBooks or Xero for that.
- No inventory management. You’ll need a separate tool like RestockPro or InventoryLab.
- No sales tax filing. You’ll need TaxJar or Avalara on top.
- Pricing starts at $19/month for up to 200 orders. Scales to $99/month for 5,000 orders. If you sell more, you’ll pay custom pricing.

Who it’s for: Sellers who want hands-off reconciliation and already use QuickBooks or Xero.

### 2. QuickBooks Online with Amazon integration
QuickBooks Online is the default small-business accounting tool. The Amazon integration pulls in sales, fees, and refunds, but it’s not as detailed as A2X.

The good:
- Full accounting suite: invoicing, payroll, bank feeds, reporting.
- Sales tax center tracks nexus and generates returns.
- Inventory tracking (in Plus plan) syncs with Amazon quantities.
- Mobile app lets you snap receipts for expenses.

The bad:
- The Amazon integration misses some fees (like long-term storage) and doesn’t split entries by product.
- Inventory tracking is basic. No reorder points or supplier lead times.
- No COGS tracking unless you enter costs manually.
- Pricing starts at $30/month for Simple Start, but you’ll need Plus ($90/month) for inventory and bill pay.

Who it’s for: Sellers who want an all-in-one tool and don’t mind some manual work.

### 3. Xero with A2X
Xero is QuickBooks’ main competitor. It’s cleaner, but the Amazon integration is weaker. Most sellers pair it with A2X.

The good:
- Unlimited users in all plans. QuickBooks charges extra for additional users.
- Strong bank reconciliation. Matches transactions faster than QuickBooks.
- Fixed asset management. Useful if you buy equipment for prep centers.
- Pricing starts at $15/month for Early, but you’ll need Growing ($42/month) for bills and bank feeds.

The bad:
- No native Amazon integration. You must use A2X or enter data manually.
- Inventory tracking is basic. No bundles or kitting.
- Sales tax is US-only. No support for VAT or other international taxes.

Who it’s for: Sellers who prefer Xero’s interface and don’t mind adding A2X.

### 4. Taxomate
Taxomate is A2X’s cheaper cousin. It does the same thing—split Amazon settlements into journal entries—but with fewer features.

The good:
- Half the price of A2X. Starts at $9/month for 200 orders.
- Supports QuickBooks Online, Xero, and Wave.
- Handles multiple marketplaces.

The bad:
- No product-level entries. You’ll see total sales, not SKU profitability.
- No automatic reconciliation. You’ll need to match entries manually.
- No inventory sync. You’ll need a separate tool.
- No sales tax support.

Who it’s for: Budget-conscious sellers who only need basic reconciliation.

## Where each tool falls short

A2X: No inventory management. You’ll need a separate tool, and the two won’t talk to each other. I’ve seen sellers over-order because their accounting tool didn’t sync with their inventory tool.

QuickBooks Online: The Amazon integration is shallow. It pulls in sales but misses some fees, so your P&L will be off by a few percent. Not enough to notice at first, but enough to cause headaches during tax season.

Xero: No native Amazon support. You’ll rely on A2X, which adds complexity. If A2X has an outage, your books stop updating.

Taxomate: No product-level data. You’ll know your total sales, but not which products are profitable. For a solo seller, that’s fine. For a team, it’s a dealbreaker.

## The rough edges no one mentions

1. Amazon’s settlement reports change without warning. A2X and Taxomate update their parsers, but there’s always a lag. I’ve seen sellers scramble when Amazon added a new fee type and their accounting tool didn’t recognize it.

2. Multi-currency is a mess. Amazon settles in your bank’s currency, but the reports are in the marketplace currency. If you sell in the US and UK, you’ll have USD and GBP transactions in the same report. Most tools handle this poorly.

3. Inventory valuation is guesswork. Amazon’s FBA reports don’t include unit costs, so you’ll either enter them manually or use FIFO/LIFO assumptions. Neither is perfect.

4. Sales tax is still your problem. Amazon collects and remits tax in most states, but you still need to file returns. QuickBooks and Xero can generate returns, but they won’t tell you if Amazon missed a state.

## What to do next

If you’re a solo seller with under 500 orders/month, start with Taxomate and QuickBooks Simple Start. It’s cheap and covers the basics.

If you’re a team or sell more than 500 orders/month, use A2X with QuickBooks Online or Xero. Add an inventory tool like RestockPro or InventoryLab.

If you sell internationally, pick Xero. It handles multi-currency better than QuickBooks.

Avoid tools that promise “Amazon integration” but don’t split settlements. You’ll spend more time fixing entries than you save.
