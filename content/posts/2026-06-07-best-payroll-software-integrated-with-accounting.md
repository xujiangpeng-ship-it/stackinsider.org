Gusto’s “Simple” plan caps you at 50 employees—after that, you’re automatically upgraded to their “Plus” tier at $80/month base + $12 per person. That’s a 60% price jump overnight, and no one warns you until you hit the limit. If you’re scaling fast, this isn’t just a budget surprise; it’s a workflow disruption because the migration to the new tier isn’t instant—expect a 24-48 hour sync delay for payroll and accounting data.

## What Sets It Apart (and Where It Doesn’t)

Gusto, QuickBooks Payroll, and Rippling all promise “seamless” accounting integration, but the reality depends on which accounting software you’re already using. Gusto’s two-way sync with QuickBooks Online is the most mature—it pushes payroll journal entries, tax liabilities, and contractor payments without manual mapping. QuickBooks Payroll, predictably, does this too, but only if you’re on QuickBooks Online Advanced ($200/month). Rippling’s sync with Xero and NetSuite is newer; as of May 2026, users still report occasional mismatches in tax codes when running multi-state payroll, something the support team acknowledges but hasn’t fully resolved.

The real standout feature isn’t the sync itself—it’s how each tool handles corrections. Gusto lets you edit a payroll after submission and automatically updates the accounting journal entry. QuickBooks Payroll locks the payroll after approval, forcing you to void and re-run it, which creates duplicate entries in your books. Rippling sits in the middle: you can edit, but the correction syncs as a separate line item, cluttering your general ledger. For teams that make frequent adjustments (think hourly workers with last-minute overtime), this isn’t just annoying—it’s a reconciliation headache.

Mobile apps are another weak spot. Gusto’s app lets you run payroll from your phone, but you can’t approve timecards or view accounting reports. QuickBooks Payroll’s app is essentially a read-only dashboard. Rippling’s mobile experience is the most functional—you can approve time, run payroll, and even pull up basic P&L statements—but it lacks offline mode, a dealbreaker for field teams or remote locations with spotty service.

## What You’ll Actually Pay

Here’s how the pricing shakes out for a 50-person company with basic payroll, time tracking, and accounting sync:

|--------------------|--------------------|----------------|--------------------|-------------------------|---------------------------------|
| Gusto (Plus)       | $80                | $12            | $680               | Month-to-month          | $6/employee for time tracking   |
| QuickBooks Payroll | $80                | $8             | $480               | Annual contract         | $200 setup fee for new accounts |
| Rippling           | $35                | $8             | $435               | Month-to-month          | $50/month for API access        |
| OnPay              | $40                | $6             | $340               | Month-to-month          | None                            |

Gusto’s pricing looks competitive until you add time tracking—suddenly, you’re paying $980/month. QuickBooks Payroll’s $200 setup fee is buried in the terms and only surfaces during checkout. Rippling’s API access fee is a gotcha for teams that need custom integrations (e.g., syncing with a niche HRIS). OnPay is the only one with no hidden fees, but it lacks native accounting sync with Xero or NetSuite—you’ll need to export CSV files and manually import them.

## The Rough Edges

Gusto’s customer support is hit or miss. Their chatbot is fast, but escalating to a human can take 24-48 hours during tax season (G2 score: 4.2/5 as of June 2026, down from 4.5 in 2025). QuickBooks Payroll’s support is outsourced, and users report inconsistent answers on tax filings—something Intuit’s own community forums confirm. Rippling’s support is the most responsive, but their documentation is sparse; for example, there’s no clear guide on how to handle retroactive pay increases in a way that syncs correctly with accounting.

Integration friction is real. Gusto’s sync with QuickBooks Desktop is one-way only (push from Gusto to QB), and it doesn’t support class tracking. Rippling’s NetSuite integration requires a $1,500 implementation fee for custom mapping. QuickBooks Payroll’s sync with Xero is still in beta and only available to select users. If you’re not on QuickBooks Online, you’re essentially locked out of the “seamless” experience.

## Where It Falls Short

For companies with international teams, none of these tools are ideal. Gusto supports contractors in 120+ countries, but it doesn’t handle local tax filings—you’ll need a separate PEO. Rippling’s global payroll is limited to 10 countries, and QuickBooks Payroll doesn’t support international payroll at all. If you’re hiring in the EU or APAC, you’ll need to pair these tools with a global payroll provider like Deel or Remote, which adds complexity and cost.

For teams using niche accounting software (e.g., Sage Intacct, FreshBooks), the options narrow further. Gusto and Rippling offer CSV exports, but you’ll spend hours each month manually reconciling data. QuickBooks Payroll doesn’t integrate with anything outside the Intuit ecosystem. OnPay is the most flexible here—it supports direct sync with FreshBooks and Sage 50—but lacks advanced features like multi-entity payroll.

## Who Should Use What

If you’re a 10-50 person team on QuickBooks Online and want the easiest setup, Gusto is the best fit. The sync works, the mobile app covers the basics, and the pricing is transparent (once you account for the time-tracking add-on). Just be prepared for the 50-employee price cliff.

For teams already on QuickBooks Online Advanced, QuickBooks Payroll is the obvious choice—no sync issues, and the pricing is predictable. But if you’re not on Advanced, the lack of two-way sync with other accounting tools makes it a non-starter.

Rippling is the best pick for mid-market teams (50-500 employees) that need flexibility. The ability to run payroll, manage HR, and sync with multiple accounting systems (including NetSuite and Xero) is unmatched. The trade-off? Higher implementation costs and occasional sync hiccups.

OnPay is the budget-friendly option for small teams that don’t need advanced accounting integrations. It’s simple, affordable, and reliable—but don’t expect it to scale with complex needs.

If you’re hiring internationally, pair any of these tools with a global payroll provider. None of them handle local compliance well enough to stand alone.
