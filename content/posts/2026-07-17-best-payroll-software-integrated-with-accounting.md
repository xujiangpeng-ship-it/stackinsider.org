The last time I ran payroll for a 25-person agency, the accounting integration promised on the sales page turned into a 45-minute export-import dance every payday. The CSV never matched the bank feed, and I had to manually adjust journal entries for tax withholdings that the system misclassified. That’s why “integrated” is a red flag until you verify it actually means one-click sync with your general ledger.

Below is what I’ve learned from implementing, migrating, and troubleshooting payroll tools for teams between 5 and 250 employees. I’ll cover the three options that reliably sync with accounting software, what you’ll pay, and the daily workflows that either save or waste time.

Gusto and Rippling let you start without a contract. ADP Run locks you in for a year and charges a $500 setup fee that’s not waived unless you negotiate. QuickBooks Payroll only works if you already use QuickBooks Online; if you don’t, add $30–$80/month for accounting.

## What sets each one apart

### Gusto
Gusto syncs with QuickBooks Online, Xero, and FreshBooks. The sync is real: one click pushes payroll journal entries, tax liabilities, and employee reimbursements into the general ledger. I’ve seen teams cut month-end close by two days because the entries match the bank feed on the first try.

The mobile app lets employees submit expenses and W-2s without calling HR. The downside is the reporting: custom reports require exporting to Excel and rebuilding formulas. If you need deep analytics, you’ll still do that outside Gusto.

### Rippling
Rippling combines payroll, HR, and IT in one system. The integration with accounting is native: no middleware, no CSV exports. It also handles global payroll for contractors in 100+ countries, which Gusto and QuickBooks don’t. The catch is the learning curve. The UI is dense, and the onboarding checklist feels overwhelming for teams under 50.

Rippling’s automation is the best I’ve seen. It auto-enrolls new hires in benefits, assigns equipment, and triggers payroll based on time-tracking approvals. If you’re scaling fast, this saves hours. If you’re stable, the extra features might feel like overkill.

### QuickBooks Payroll
QuickBooks Payroll is the only tool that lives inside your accounting software. There’s no sync because it’s the same system. This is ideal if you already use QuickBooks Online and want one less login. The downside is the payroll features are basic. No global payroll, no advanced time tracking, and the mobile app is just a read-only dashboard.

### ADP Run
ADP Run is the enterprise option that still fits mid-size teams. The accounting integration is via API or flat-file export, not native. You’ll need an IT person to map fields correctly. The reporting is robust, but the UI feels dated. I’ve seen teams stick with ADP because they’re afraid of migration, not because it’s the best fit.

## The rough edges

Gusto’s customer support is email-first. Phone support is available, but hold times average 20 minutes. Rippling’s support is faster, but the responses are scripted. QuickBooks Payroll support is hit or miss; if you get a rep who knows payroll, it’s great. If not, you’ll be transferred twice.

Rippling’s global payroll is powerful, but the tax engine is still catching up. I’ve seen misclassified withholdings for remote employees in three states. Gusto and QuickBooks handle domestic payroll cleanly, but neither supports international contractors.

ADP Run’s implementation is the worst. The $500 fee covers a kickoff call and a PDF guide. You’ll still need to manually enter employee data, tax IDs, and bank details. The process takes 2–3 weeks, not the 5 days ADP promises.

## Where it falls short

Gusto’s time tracking is an add-on. If you use TSheets or Homebase, you’ll pay extra. Rippling’s time tracking is built-in, but the mobile app crashes on Android 13. QuickBooks Payroll’s time tracking is basic; no geofencing or job costing.

Rippling’s pricing is opaque. The $35 base fee is for HR only. Payroll adds $8 per employee, benefits add another $6, and IT adds $5. By the time you’re done, you’re paying $20–$25 per employee, not the $8 advertised.

ADP Run’s reporting is powerful, but the interface is clunky. Custom reports require a separate module that costs $50/month. QuickBooks Payroll’s reporting is clean, but the filters are limited. You can’t run a report on overtime by department without exporting to Excel.

## Who each tool is for

Gusto is best for teams between 5 and 100 employees who want a simple, reliable payroll system that syncs with accounting. It’s not for global teams or companies that need deep HR analytics.

Rippling is for fast-growing teams that need HR, payroll, and IT in one system. It’s overkill for stable teams or those with fewer than 20 employees.

QuickBooks Payroll is for small businesses already using QuickBooks Online. It’s the easiest option if you don’t need advanced features.

ADP Run is for mid-size teams that need enterprise-grade reporting and don’t mind the higher cost and longer implementation.

## What to watch

Gusto is rolling out global payroll for contractors in late 2026. Rippling is updating its tax engine to handle multi-state withholdings more accurately. QuickBooks Payroll is adding job costing to its time-tracking module. ADP Run is redesigning its UI, but the timeline is unclear.

If you’re choosing today, pick based on your current needs. Migration is easier than it used to be, but it’s still a pain. Start with a tool that fits now, not one you’ll grow into.
