Salesforce’s jump to $150/user for AI isn’t just about features—it’s about data. Their AI models require clean, structured data, and most SMBs don’t have it. The “Data Cloud” add-on (another $50/user/month) is practically mandatory if you want Einstein to do more than spit out generic follow-up templates. HubSpot’s pricing is more predictable, but their AI features are gated behind the Enterprise plan, which starts at $120/user/month.

Zoho’s $40/user plan is the best value for small teams, but only if you’re already in their ecosystem. Their AI assistant, Zia, integrates with Zoho Desk and Zoho Books, but if you’re using QuickBooks or Slack, you’ll hit friction. Pipedrive’s AI is the weakest of the bunch—it’s essentially a wrapper around third-party tools like Gong or Chorus, which defeats the purpose of an all-in-one CRM.

## The Rough Edges

AI-powered CRMs still struggle with context. HubSpot’s AI can draft emails, but it defaults to a tone that’s either too formal or too casual—there’s no in-between. Users on Reddit’s r/sales thread (April 2026) report spending more time editing AI-generated emails than writing them from scratch. Salesforce’s Einstein Copilot has the opposite problem: it’s too aggressive with follow-ups, sometimes sending three emails in a week to the same prospect.

Then there’s the data problem. AI models are only as good as the data they’re trained on, and most CRMs don’t have enough of it. Zoho’s Zia requires at least 100 closed deals before it can accurately predict win rates. For startups or small teams, that’s a non-starter. Freshsales’ AI chatbot, Fred, has a similar limitation—it needs at least 500 customer interactions before it can handle complex queries. Until then, it’s little more than a glorified FAQ tool.

The biggest frustration? Most AI features are opt-in. Sales reps ignore them because they don’t trust the recommendations, and managers don’t enforce usage because the ROI isn’t immediate. A survey of 200 sales teams by Gartner in late 2025 found that only 38% of AI CRM features were being used consistently.

## Where It Falls Short

If you’re a support team, AI CRMs are still playing catch-up. HubSpot’s AI ticket routing is fast, but it can’t handle nuanced requests—like a customer asking for a refund because of a billing error. It defaults to the “Billing” queue, even if the issue is a product defect. Zoho Desk’s AI is better at this, but it requires manual tagging, which defeats the purpose of automation.

For enterprise teams, the biggest gap is customization. Salesforce’s AI models are powerful, but they’re also rigid. If your sales process doesn’t fit their template, you’ll spend months (and tens of thousands of dollars) tweaking it. Smaller teams fare better with HubSpot or Zoho, but they’ll hit limits as they scale.

The other missing piece? Offline functionality. None of the major CRMs have a true offline mode for their AI features. If you’re on a plane or in a dead zone, you’re back to manual data entry.

## Who Should (and Shouldn’t) Use These Tools

If you’re a sales team of 10-50 people with a predictable sales cycle, HubSpot’s AI features are the best balance of cost and functionality. The deal scoring and email drafting save time, and the pricing is transparent. Just be prepared to spend a few hours a week training the AI.

For support teams, Zoho Desk’s AI is the most practical. It’s not perfect, but it handles ticket routing and basic responses well enough to reduce response times by 20-30%. The $40/user/month price is hard to beat.

Enterprise teams with deep pockets and complex workflows will still gravitate toward Salesforce, but only if they’re willing to pay for the Data Cloud add-on and hire a consultant to set it up. The AI features are powerful, but they’re not plug-and-play.

If you’re a startup or a team under 10 people, skip the AI for now. The cost isn’t worth the marginal gains. Use a simpler CRM like Pipedrive or Freshsales, and revisit AI in a year or two when the tools are more mature.

The real winners in 2026 aren’t the teams using AI CRMs—they’re the teams using them selectively. Pick one or two AI features that solve a real pain point (like deal scoring or ticket routing), and ignore the rest. The rest is just noise.
