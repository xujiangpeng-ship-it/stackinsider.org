Most expense tracking apps promise "effortless" reconciliation, but the reality is messier. Take Expensify’s 2026 pricing update: their "Free" tier now caps at 25 receipts per month, down from 50 in 2024, and forces users into a $9/user/month plan for basic features like multi-currency support. If your team travels internationally, that’s a $108 annual surprise per employee—before you even hit the "Submit" button on your first report.

## What You’ll Actually Pay
Expense tracking apps love to bury costs in fine print. Here’s what you’re *really* signing up for in 2026:

- **Expensify**: $9/user/month for the "Collect" plan (the minimum viable tier for teams), but add-ons like advanced approval workflows or ERP integrations push costs to $25/user/month. Their "Corporate" plan, often marketed as "enterprise-ready," starts at $18/user/month—but only if you commit to 100+ users. Smaller teams get hit with a 20% premium.
- **Ramp**: Free for basic expense tracking, but their "Plus" plan ($15/user/month) is required for custom approval chains or API access. A recent G2 review (April 2026) noted that Ramp’s "free" card issuance is limited to 5 physical cards per company, after which it’s $5 per card—an unexpected cost for growing teams.
- **Zoho Expense**: $3/user/month for the "Standard" plan, but their "Premium" tier ($6/user/month) is the first to include multi-currency or travel booking. Zoho’s pricing is transparent, but their mobile app lags behind competitors in user ratings (3.8/5 on G2 as of May 2026, compared to Expensify’s 4.5).
- **Brex**: Free for startups under $1M in funding, but their "Scale" plan ($12/user/month) is mandatory for companies with 50+ employees. Brex’s hidden gotcha? Their "free" tier doesn’t include expense report exports—critical for audits or accounting handoffs.

## Features That Actually Matter
Forget the marketing fluff. These are the features that make or break your workflow:

**Receipt Auto-Capture**: Expensify’s "SmartScan" is still the gold standard—it extracts line items, taxes, and vendor details with 92% accuracy (per their 2025 whitepaper). Ramp’s version is faster but misses handwritten receipts entirely. If your team deals with paper receipts (think: conferences, client dinners), Ramp’s auto-capture is a non-starter.

**Approval Workflows**: Zoho Expense lets you build multi-level approval chains with conditional logic (e.g., "If expense > $500, route to CFO"). Expensify’s workflows are simpler but integrate natively with Slack, which matters if your team lives in chat. Brex’s approvals are tied to their card program—great if you use Brex cards, frustrating if you don’t.

**ERP Integrations**: Ramp and Expensify sync with NetSuite and QuickBooks Online in real time, but Ramp’s integration is read-only. If your accounting team needs to push adjustments back to Ramp, you’ll hit a wall. Zoho Expense plays nice with Zoho Books but struggles with third-party ERPs—expect manual CSV exports for non-Zoho setups.

**Mobile Experience**: Expensify’s mobile app lets you submit expenses offline, but it’s clunky on Android (per a Reddit thread from March 2026, users report crashes when uploading receipts in low-light conditions). Brex’s app is sleek but lacks offline mode entirely. Zoho’s app is functional but slow—expect a 2-3 second delay when switching between receipts.

## The Rough Edges
No app is perfect. Here’s what users complain about in 2026:

- **Expensify’s AI is overzealous**: Their "Expense Assistant" flags personal expenses as "potential duplicates" 30% of the time (per a 2025 user survey). If your team submits a lot of similar expenses (e.g., monthly software subscriptions), expect false positives.
- **Ramp’s customer support is slow**: Their average response time for "Plus" plan users is 48 hours (per a G2 review from February 2026). If your team relies on quick fixes (e.g., a card lock during travel), this is a dealbreaker.
- **Zoho’s reporting is rigid**: Their pre-built reports are limited to basic categories (e.g., "Meals," "Travel"). Custom reports require manual SQL queries—fine for data teams, frustrating for everyone else.
- **Brex’s "free" tier is a teaser**: Their startup plan excludes expense report exports, which means you’ll need to upgrade before your first audit. Many users don’t realize this until it’s too late.

## Where It Falls Short
The biggest gap in 2026’s expense tracking apps? **Context**. None of these tools integrate deeply with project management or CRM systems. For example:
- If you bill expenses to clients, you’ll need to manually tag them in your expense app *and* your time-tracking tool (e.g., Harvest, Toggl).
- If your team tracks expenses by project (common in agencies or consulting), you’ll need to export reports and reconcile them in Excel or a BI tool like Power BI.
- Ramp and Brex tie expenses to their card programs, which works if you use their cards exclusively. If your team uses a mix of corporate cards, personal cards, and reimbursements, you’ll spend hours reconciling discrepancies.

## Who Should Use What
- **Startups (under 50 employees)**: Ramp’s free tier is the best deal, but only if you use their cards. If you need multi-currency or offline mobile support, Expensify’s $9/user/month plan is worth the cost.
- **Mid-sized teams (50-500 employees)**: Expensify or Zoho Expense. Expensify for teams that prioritize mobile and AI, Zoho for those on a tight budget or already using Zoho’s ecosystem.
- **Enterprises (500+ employees)**: Expensify’s "Corporate" plan or a custom ERP integration. Brex’s user caps make it impractical at scale.
- **Agencies/consulting firms**: None of these tools are perfect, but Expensify’s Slack integration and client billing features come closest. Expect to supplement with manual exports.

If you’re evaluating expense tracking apps in 2026, start with your team’s workflow—not the feature list. The "best" app is the one that solves your specific pain points (e.g., multi-currency, approval chains, mobile receipt capture) without introducing new ones. And always, *always* read the pricing fine print.
