Freelancers don’t need enterprise dashboards or Gantt charts that look like airport runways. You need something that handles three things: tracking time, sending invoices, and not making you feel like you’re herding cats. Most tools fail at least one of these.

I’ve set up, migrated, or abandoned PM software for over 50 solo operators in the last two years. Here’s what actually matters when you’re choosing a tool for yourself.

## What freelancers actually need from a PM tool

Most feature lists read like they were written for a 50-person agency. Freelancers care about:

- Time tracking that doesn’t feel like a timesheet from 1998.
- Invoicing that doesn’t require exporting to another app.
- Client access that doesn’t expose your entire workspace.
- Mobile apps that work offline because coffee shops still have spotty Wi-Fi.

Anything beyond that is either a nice-to-have or a distraction.

## Where each tool shines (and where it doesn’t)

### ClickUp
ClickUp is the Swiss Army knife that tries to do everything. It succeeds at customization but fails at simplicity.

**Good:** You can build a workspace that matches how you think. Time tracking integrates with tasks. Client portals let you share only what you want.

**Bad:** The mobile app crashes if you have more than 50 tasks. Offline mode is read-only. Invoicing requires a Zapier integration that breaks every few months.

I’ve seen freelancers spend weeks tweaking ClickUp only to abandon it because the maintenance became a second job.

### Notion
Notion is a blank notebook. That’s both its strength and its weakness.

**Good:** You can design exactly the workflow you want. Databases, wikis, task lists—it all lives in one place. Mobile app works offline and syncs when you’re back online.

**Bad:** No native time tracking or invoicing. You’ll need to integrate with Toggl or Harvest, which adds cost and complexity. Client access is all-or-nothing; you can’t hide internal notes.

Best for freelancers who enjoy building systems more than doing client work.

### Trello
Trello is the simplest option, but simplicity comes at a cost.

**Good:** Free plan is usable for basic task tracking. Mobile app is fast and works offline. Power-ups add functionality without clutter.

**Bad:** No time tracking or invoicing. Client access requires sharing boards, which exposes your entire workflow. No reporting—you’ll need to export to Excel if you want to analyze your time.

Good for freelancers who just need a digital to-do list, not a full business hub.

### HoneyBook
HoneyBook is built for service providers, not developers or designers.

**Good:** Time tracking, invoicing, and contracts are all in one place. Client portal is polished and professional. Automations handle follow-ups and reminders.

**Bad:** $34/month is steep for solo operators. Mobile app doesn’t work offline. Customization is limited—you’re stuck with their workflow.

Best for freelancers who sell packages (e.g., photographers, coaches) rather than hourly work.

### Toggl Plan
Toggl Plan is a stripped-down version of Toggl Track with a visual timeline.

**Good:** Time tracking is simple and accurate. Timeline view helps with scheduling. Free plan is generous (up to 5 clients).

**Bad:** No invoicing or client portal. Mobile app is read-only. Reporting is basic—you’ll need to export to Excel for anything detailed.

Good for freelancers who live in their calendar and just need a way to track time against tasks.

### Bonsai
Bonsai is the closest thing to an all-in-one tool for freelancers.

**Good:** Time tracking, invoicing, contracts, and client portal all work together. Mobile app works offline. Automations handle proposals, contracts, and payments.

**Bad:** $24/month is expensive for new freelancers. Customization is limited—you’re stuck with their templates. No native project management beyond basic tasks.

Best for freelancers who want to automate their admin work and don’t mind paying for it.

## What you’ll actually pay

Most tools advertise low monthly rates but hit you with hidden costs:

- ClickUp: $9/month for time tracking, but invoicing requires a $20/month Zapier plan.
- Notion: $10/month for the Pro plan, but time tracking and invoicing require separate tools (Toggl + Wave = $15/month).
- Trello: $5/month for the Premium plan, but time tracking and invoicing require integrations (Toggl + Paymo = $20/month).
- HoneyBook: $34/month, no hidden costs, but expensive for solo operators.
- Toggl Plan: $9/month, but invoicing requires a separate tool (Wave = free, but manual).
- Bonsai: $24/month, no hidden costs, but templates are rigid.

Freelancers on a budget should expect to spend $15-25/month for a full setup, not the $5-10 advertised.

## The rough edges

No tool is perfect. Here’s what users complain about:

- ClickUp: Mobile app is slow and buggy. Offline mode is read-only.
- Notion: No native time tracking or invoicing. Client access is all-or-nothing.
- Trello: No time tracking or invoicing. Client access exposes your entire workflow.
- HoneyBook: Expensive for solo operators. Mobile app doesn’t work offline.
- Toggl Plan: No invoicing or client portal. Mobile app is read-only.
- Bonsai: Expensive for new freelancers. Templates are rigid.

## What sets Bonsai apart

Bonsai is the only tool that handles time tracking, invoicing, and client access in one place. The mobile app works offline, which is rare. Automations save time on proposals, contracts, and payments.

It’s not perfect—$24/month is steep, and customization is limited—but it’s the closest thing to an all-in-one tool for freelancers.

## Who should use what

- **New freelancers on a budget:** Trello + Toggl Track + Wave. Cheap, simple, but manual.
- **Freelancers who love systems:** Notion + Toggl Track. Flexible, but requires setup.
- **Freelancers who want automation:** Bonsai. Expensive, but saves time on admin.
- **Service providers (coaches, photographers):** HoneyBook. Polished, but pricey.

If you’re just starting, try Trello first. It’s free, simple, and you can always migrate later. If you’re tired of juggling multiple tools, Bonsai is worth the cost.

The best PM for freelancers isn’t the one with the most features. It’s the one you’ll actually use.
