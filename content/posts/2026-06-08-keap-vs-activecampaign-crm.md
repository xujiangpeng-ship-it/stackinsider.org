Keap’s pricing page looks simple: $159/month for 1,500 contacts. What it doesn’t tell you? That’s the *annual* rate. Switch to month-to-month and the same plan jumps to $199. ActiveCampaign, on the other hand, lets you cancel anytime—but buries its CRM features behind a separate “Sales” add-on that costs another $29/month per user. Neither tool is transparent about the real cost of scaling, and both assume you’ll grow into their pricing before you’ve even onboarded.

## What You’ll Actually Pay

Keap’s entry plan starts at $159/month (annual only) for 1,500 contacts and 2 users. Each additional user costs $29/month. ActiveCampaign’s “Plus” plan, which includes basic CRM, starts at $49/month for 1,000 contacts and 3 users. But if you want deal pipelines, lead scoring, or sales automation, you’ll need the “Professional” plan at $149/month—plus the $29/month Sales add-on for each user.

Here’s how the costs stack up for a team of 5 with 5,000 contacts:

| Feature                     | Keap (Pro)       | ActiveCampaign (Professional + Sales) |
|-----------------------------|------------------|---------------------------------------|
| Base price                  | $249/month       | $149/month                           |
| Per-user add-on             | $29 x 3 = $87    | $29 x 5 = $145                       |
| Total                       | $336/month       | $294/month                           |
| Contract requirement        | Annual only      | Month-to-month                       |
| Migration fee (if applicable)| $999 (one-time)  | None                                 |

Keap’s annual contract isn’t just a billing preference—it’s a lock-in. Cancel early and you’ll owe the remaining balance. ActiveCampaign doesn’t force contracts, but its pricing model punishes teams that need both marketing and sales features. The Sales add-on, for example, is required to use the CRM’s deal pipelines, even though the feature is listed under “CRM” on the website.

## Features That Actually Matter

### Automation: Keap’s Strength, ActiveCampaign’s Nuance
Keap’s automation builder is visual and intuitive, with pre-built templates for common workflows like abandoned carts or lead follow-ups. It’s designed for small businesses that don’t have a dedicated marketing ops person. ActiveCampaign’s automation is more powerful but requires a steeper learning curve. Its “conditional splits” and “goal-based triggers” let you create complex journeys, but teams often report spending hours debugging workflows that don’t fire as expected.

One real-world example: A client in the home services industry used Keap to automate a 7-day follow-up sequence for quote requests. The workflow included SMS reminders, email templates, and a task for the sales rep to call on day 3. It took 20 minutes to set up. The same workflow in ActiveCampaign required custom fields, multiple “if/else” conditions, and a workaround for the SMS integration, which doesn’t natively support two-way messaging.

### CRM: ActiveCampaign’s Flexibility vs. Keap’s Simplicity
ActiveCampaign’s CRM is built for sales teams that need customization. You can create unlimited deal pipelines, custom fields, and even automate deal stage changes based on email engagement. Keap’s CRM, by contrast, is simpler but more rigid. It includes a single deal pipeline with fixed stages, and while you can add custom fields, the interface feels cluttered when you exceed 10-15.

A key limitation: Keap’s mobile app doesn’t support deal management. Sales reps can view contacts and send emails, but they can’t update deal stages or log calls from the field. ActiveCampaign’s mobile app, while not perfect, at least lets you move deals through the pipeline.

### Reporting: Where Both Tools Fall Short
Neither tool excels at reporting. Keap’s dashboards are basic, with pre-built reports for email performance and deal conversion rates. ActiveCampaign offers more customization, but its reporting interface is slow and often times out when pulling large datasets. A G2 review from March 2026 noted, “Generating a report for more than 10,000 contacts crashes the browser tab 60% of the time.”

## The Rough Edges

### Keap’s Hidden Costs
Beyond the annual contract, Keap charges for features that should be standard. Want to send SMS messages? That’s $0.015 per message, plus a $10/month fee for the integration. Need to migrate from another tool? Keap’s “concierge migration” starts at $999. ActiveCampaign includes SMS in its higher-tier plans and offers free migration for up to 5,000 contacts.

### ActiveCampaign’s Integration Friction
ActiveCampaign’s API is powerful, but its native integrations often feel half-baked. The Shopify integration, for example, doesn’t sync customer tags in real time, which breaks automation workflows for e-commerce teams. Users in the ActiveCampaign community forum frequently report having to use Zapier or Make as a workaround, adding another layer of cost and complexity.

### Onboarding: Keap’s Handholding vs. ActiveCampaign’s DIY Approach
Keap includes a 30-minute onboarding call with every plan, which is helpful for teams new to CRM software. ActiveCampaign offers onboarding, but it’s only included in the Enterprise plan ($259/month). For smaller teams, the learning curve is steep—expect to spend 10-15 hours watching tutorials and testing workflows before feeling comfortable.

## Where It Shines (and Where It Doesn’t)

Keap is the better choice for small businesses that want an all-in-one tool with minimal setup. Its automation templates and built-in invoicing (a feature ActiveCampaign lacks) make it ideal for solopreneurs or teams with simple sales processes. But if you need customization or plan to scale beyond 10,000 contacts, the rigid structure and hidden costs become frustrating.

ActiveCampaign is the stronger option for teams that need flexibility and don’t mind a steeper learning curve. Its CRM and automation features are more advanced, and the lack of an annual contract is a major plus. But the Sales add-on and integration limitations make it less ideal for e-commerce or field sales teams.

## Who Should Choose What?

If you’re a small business with a straightforward sales process and want a tool that “just works,” Keap is the safer bet—just be prepared to commit to an annual contract. If you’re a growing team that needs customization and can invest time in setup, ActiveCampaign’s flexibility is worth the effort. But if you’re in e-commerce or rely heavily on SMS, neither tool is perfect—you’ll likely need a third-party integration to fill the gaps.
