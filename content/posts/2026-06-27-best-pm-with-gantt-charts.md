The last time I migrated a 50-person team off spreadsheets, the Gantt chart in their new tool broke on day three. Dependencies vanished, milestones shifted, and the timeline view refused to scroll past week two. That tool looked great in demos but failed the first real test: keeping a complex project visible without manual fixes every morning.

Gantt charts matter because they show dependencies at a glance. But not all tools handle them the same way. Some treat them as a checkbox feature, others build the whole product around them. Here’s what I’ve learned after setting up and running projects in four of the most common options.

## What you’ll actually pay

Pricing changes often, but the structure rarely does. These are the current public rates as of June 2026, pulled directly from vendor sites.

|---------------|-----------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| ClickUp       | Yes       | $7                            | $12                           | Yes                 | Yes (all plans) |
| Monday.com    | No        | $8                            | $10                           | Yes                 | Yes (Pro+)      |
| Smartsheet    | No        | $7                            | $25                           | Yes                 | Yes (all plans) |

ClickUp and Smartsheet include Gantt charts in every plan. Monday.com gates them behind the Pro tier, which starts at $10 per user. TeamGantt is the only one built specifically for Gantt charts, so the price reflects that focus.

Hidden costs show up in two places. First, integrations. Smartsheet charges extra for premium connectors like Salesforce or Jira. Second, storage. ClickUp’s free tier caps file uploads at 100MB per file, which is fine for documents but not for large design assets or video walkthroughs.

## Where each tool shines

### ClickUp
The Gantt view is fast. Really fast. Zooming in and out, dragging tasks, adjusting dependencies—it all responds instantly, even with 500+ tasks. That speed matters when you’re trying to rebalance a timeline during a live meeting.

Dependencies work the way you expect. Finish-to-start, start-to-start, finish-to-finish, and start-to-finish are all there. You can set lag time in days or hours, which is useful for tasks like “start QA two days after development hands off.”

The real advantage is flexibility. ClickUp lets you switch between Gantt, list, board, and calendar views without losing context. If the Gantt chart feels overwhelming, you can drill into a single sprint in board view, then jump back to the timeline. That fluidity keeps teams from getting stuck in one perspective.

### Monday.com
Monday.com treats Gantt charts as one view among many, not the centerpiece. That’s good if your team already lives in boards or lists. The Gantt view is clean, with color-coding by status, owner, or priority. You can group tasks by phase, department, or custom fields, which makes it easy to see bottlenecks at a glance.

Automation is the standout feature. You can set rules like “when a task moves to ‘Blocked,’ notify the project lead and add a comment.” Those small triggers reduce manual check-ins. The downside is that the Gantt view itself doesn’t support automation—you can’t trigger a timeline shift based on a task update.

### Smartsheet
Smartsheet looks like Excel but behaves like a project tool. The Gantt chart is a native view, not a bolted-on feature. You can edit task details directly in the timeline, which saves time if you’re already working in grid view.

The best part is the reporting. Smartsheet lets you build dashboards that pull Gantt data into charts, tables, and summaries. You can show project health, resource allocation, or milestone progress without exporting to another tool. That’s useful for teams that need to present updates to stakeholders who don’t want to dig into the timeline.

### TeamGantt
TeamGantt is the only tool here built specifically for Gantt charts. The interface is simple: tasks on the left, timeline on the right. No extra views, no distractions. That simplicity is a strength if your team only cares about timelines.

Dependencies are easy to set and adjust. You can see the critical path with one click, which helps identify tasks that will delay the whole project if they slip. The mobile app is surprisingly good—you can update task status, add comments, and even adjust dates from your phone.

## The rough edges

### ClickUp
The learning curve is steep. ClickUp has views for everything: docs, goals, whiteboards, forms, chat. That flexibility is powerful but overwhelming. Teams I’ve worked with spend the first two weeks trying to turn off features they don’t need. The Gantt chart itself is solid, but the rest of the product can feel like a Swiss Army knife when you only need a screwdriver.

### Monday.com
The Gantt view is limited. You can’t set dependencies between tasks in different boards, which forces you to keep everything in one board or lose the timeline view. That’s a problem for larger projects that need separate boards for different teams or phases.

### Smartsheet
Performance slows down with large sheets. I’ve seen Smartsheet grids with 10,000+ rows take 10-15 seconds to load. The Gantt view struggles too—zooming or scrolling can feel sluggish. If your project has more than 500 tasks, expect delays.

### TeamGantt
It’s expensive. The starter plan is $19 per user, which is more than double ClickUp or Monday.com. That price buys you a focused tool, but it’s hard to justify if you need more than Gantt charts. The lack of built-in docs, forms, or automation means you’ll need other tools to fill the gaps.

## Who each tool is for

ClickUp works best for teams that want one tool for everything. If you’re replacing Asana, Trello, and Notion with a single platform, ClickUp’s Gantt chart is a strong contender. It’s also a good fit for remote teams that need flexibility—you can start with a simple board view and add the Gantt chart later as projects get more complex.

Monday.com is for teams that already use boards and want to add a timeline view. The Gantt chart is good enough for most projects, but it’s not the main focus. If your team lives in Kanban and only needs a Gantt chart for occasional reporting, Monday.com is a safe choice.

Smartsheet is for teams that love Excel but need project management features. The Gantt chart is robust, and the reporting is the best of the bunch. It’s a good fit for operations teams, finance, or anyone who needs to present project data in a polished way.

TeamGantt is for teams that only care about timelines. If you’re managing a construction project, event, or any initiative where dependencies are the main concern, TeamGantt is the most straightforward option. The simplicity comes at a cost, but it’s worth it if Gantt charts are your top priority.

## What to watch for

ClickUp is rolling out AI features that could change the game. The latest update includes an AI assistant that can generate task dependencies based on natural language descriptions. That could save hours of manual setup, but it’s still in beta—expect some rough edges.

Monday.com is pushing deeper into automation. The next update is supposed to include Gantt-specific triggers, like “when a task is delayed, update all dependent tasks.” If that works, it could solve one of the biggest frustrations with their current setup.

Smartsheet is working on performance improvements. The team has acknowledged the slowdowns with large sheets and is testing a new backend. If you’re managing big projects, keep an eye on their release notes.

TeamGantt is the only one not chasing extra features. They’re doubling down on Gantt charts, which means the tool will stay simple but won’t add the extras you might need later.

If you’re still unsure, start with ClickUp or Monday.com. Both offer free tiers or trials, so you can test the Gantt chart with real data before committing. Smartsheet and TeamGantt are better for specific use cases—Excel power users and timeline purists, respectively. Pick the one that matches how your team already works, not the one with the flashiest demo.
