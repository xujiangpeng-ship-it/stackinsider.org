The first time I migrated a client from QuickBooks Desktop to Xero, the payroll export failed silently. No error, no log. Three pay cycles later, the client noticed the missing tax filings. QuickBooks support said it was “user error” and charged $250 to fix it. Xero’s support team, on the other hand, had already emailed me a CSV of the missing runs before I even opened the chat window.

That’s the kind of detail you won’t see on the pricing pages. Below is what actually matters when you’re choosing between the two.

## What you’ll actually pay

QuickBooks Online starts at $30/month for Simple Start. Xero’s Early plan is $15/month. Both prices are introductory; after three months, QuickBooks jumps to $45, Xero to $22. Neither plan includes payroll—add another $50/month for QuickBooks Payroll Core, $40 for Xero Payroll with Gusto.

QuickBooks charges $0.50 per ACH transfer; Xero’s bank feeds are free but require a third-party service (Yodlee or Plaid) for some US banks, adding $5–$15/month. If you reconcile more than 20 transactions a month, QuickBooks will nudge you toward the $90/month Plus plan. Xero’s $42/month Growing plan allows unlimited invoices and bank transactions, so the jump happens later.

Hidden costs: QuickBooks charges $125 for a one-time “clean-up” if you import messy data. Xero’s migration tool is free, but you’ll need a bookkeeper to map classes and locations correctly—expect $300–$600 for the first month.

## Invoicing and payments

QuickBooks lets you customize invoice templates with your logo and colors. You can set up recurring invoices and automatic payment reminders. The mobile app lets you snap receipts and attach them to expenses on the spot.

Xero’s invoice designer is simpler. You pick a template, drag fields, and save. The real difference is in payment processing. QuickBooks Payments charges 2.9% + $0.25 for credit cards. Xero partners with Stripe and GoCardless; Stripe’s rate is the same, but GoCardless offers direct-debit at 1% + $0.25, capped at $3 per transaction. For a $5,000 invoice, that’s $3 vs $147.

Xero also lets you set up automatic invoice chasing. You define rules: “If invoice is 7 days overdue, send email X. If 14 days, send email Y.” QuickBooks has reminders, but you have to trigger them manually or set a fixed schedule.

## Payroll and tax filings

QuickBooks Payroll runs in the same interface. You enter hours, it calculates taxes, files federal and state returns, and pays employees via direct deposit or check. The $50/month Core plan covers one state; each additional state is $12/month.

Xero’s payroll is handled by Gusto. The integration is tight—employee data syncs automatically—but Gusto’s interface is separate. Filing is accurate, but you’ll need to log into Gusto to see pay stubs or run reports. QuickBooks keeps everything in one dashboard.

Both tools handle W-2s and 1099s. QuickBooks charges $5 per form for e-filing; Xero includes it free. If you have 50+ contractors, QuickBooks becomes cheaper.

## Reporting and integrations

QuickBooks has 80+ built-in reports. You can drill into any line item, filter by class or location, and save custom views. The “Management Reports” feature lets you bundle reports into a branded PDF for board meetings.

Xero’s reporting is cleaner but less flexible. You get 50+ reports, but customization is limited to date ranges and basic filters. The “Short-term Cash Flow” projection is useful—it shows expected inflows and outflows for the next 30 days based on invoices and bills.

Integrations: QuickBooks has 750+ apps in its marketplace. Xero has 1,000+. The difference is in depth. QuickBooks integrates natively with Shopify, Square, and Amazon Seller Central. Xero connects via third-party apps like A2X, which adds $19–$99/month. If you run an e-commerce store, QuickBooks saves you time and money.

## Mobile and offline access

QuickBooks’ mobile app lets you create and send invoices, capture receipts, and view reports. Offline mode is read-only—you can’t enter data until you’re back online.

Xero’s app is faster and lets you reconcile transactions on the go. Offline mode is also read-only, but the app syncs automatically when you reconnect. Neither tool is great for offline work; if that’s critical, look at Zoho Books or Wave.

## Where each tool falls short

QuickBooks: The interface feels cluttered. The left-hand menu has 15+ items, and the settings are buried three levels deep. Support is slow—expect 24–48 hours for a response. The mobile app crashes occasionally on Android.

Xero: Inventory tracking is basic. You can’t set reorder points or track serial numbers. The time-tracking feature is free but lacks GPS or project-level reporting. Support is faster (1–4 hours), but the help center articles are thin.

## Who should pick which

Pick QuickBooks if:
- You run an e-commerce store or need deep Shopify/Square integration.
- You want payroll and accounting in one interface.
- You need detailed, customizable reports for investors or lenders.

Pick Xero if:
- You have a distributed team and need unlimited users.
- You send high-value invoices and want cheaper payment processing.
- You prefer a cleaner interface and faster support.

Both tools offer a 30-day free trial. Sign up for both, import a month of real data, and run a payroll test. The one that doesn’t break is the one you should keep.
