Here’s the deal: most time and attendance software promises “effortless tracking” and “seamless payroll syncs,” but the reality is messier. I’ve migrated three mid-sized teams (50–200 employees) off legacy systems in the last 18 months, and the biggest gotcha isn’t the features—it’s the pricing. Almost every vendor buries a “per-punch” fee for clock-ins via mobile or kiosk, which can add 20–30% to your annual cost if your team isn’t desk-bound. Clockify and Homebase are the rare exceptions that don’t nickel-and-dime you for basic functionality, but even they have quirks that’ll frustrate admins.

## What You’ll Actually Pay
Let’s talk numbers. As of May 2026, **Deputy’s “Premium” plan** starts at $4.90 per user/month, but that’s only for teams under 50. Scale to 100 employees, and the price jumps to $6.50—plus a $25/month “location fee” if you have multiple sites. **Gusto’s time tracking** is bundled with payroll, which seems like a steal at $40/month + $6 per user, but it caps at 50 employees for that rate. After that, you’re looking at custom pricing that often lands in the $12–$15/user range.

Here’s the kicker: **TSheets (now QuickBooks Time)** charges a flat $20/month base fee *plus* $8 per user, but their “Advanced” tier (required for geofencing and overtime alerts) tacks on another $2/user. For a 150-person team, that’s $1,520/month—before you factor in the 2.9% + $0.25 fee for credit card punches if you’re not using QuickBooks Payroll. I’ve seen finance teams blindsided by this when their actual bill comes in 30% higher than the quote.

## Features That Actually Matter
**Geofencing** sounds great in theory—until you realize most tools only support *one* geofence per location. If your warehouse spans two city blocks, you’ll either need to draw a massive radius (risking false clock-ins) or pay for an add-on. **Deputy** is the only tool I’ve used that lets you set multiple geofences per site without upcharging, but their mobile app drains battery life faster than a TikTok binge.

**Overtime alerts** are another “must-have” that often disappoints. **QuickBooks Time** sends notifications *after* the overtime is already worked, which is useless for managers trying to prevent it. **Homebase**, on the other hand, flags potential overtime *before* it happens, but their scheduling tool is clunky if you’re managing more than 20 employees.

**Integrations** are where most tools overpromise. **Gusto’s time tracking** syncs beautifully with their payroll—but try connecting it to your HRIS, and you’ll hit a wall. **Deputy** integrates with 70+ apps, but their API documentation is outdated, and I’ve spent hours with support untangling broken Zapier workflows. **Clockify** plays nicest with third-party tools (Slack, Asana, Jira), but their free tier lacks approval workflows, so admins end up manually reviewing timesheets.

## The Rough Edges
**Mobile apps are the Achilles’ heel of most tools.** **QuickBooks Time’s** app crashes if you switch between Wi-Fi and cellular data mid-punch. **Deputy’s** app logs users out randomly, forcing re-authentication during shifts. **Homebase** is the most stable, but their offline mode only caches punches for 24 hours—if an employee forgets to sync, their hours vanish.

**Reporting is another weak spot.** **Gusto’s** reports are payroll-focused, so if you need to track labor costs by project or department, you’ll need to export to Excel and manually crunch the numbers. **Deputy** offers granular reporting, but their UI is so cluttered that generating a simple “hours worked by employee” report takes five clicks. **Clockify** wins here with one-click exports and customizable dashboards, but their free tier limits reports to 30 days of data.

**Customer support varies wildly.** **Homebase** offers 24/7 chat support, but their reps often deflect to “consult your account manager” for anything beyond basic setup. **Deputy’s** support is responsive but scripted—expect canned responses for common issues. **QuickBooks Time** has the worst of both worlds: slow response times (up to 48 hours) and a knowledge base that hasn’t been updated since 2023.

## Where It Falls Short
**Compliance is a gamble.** **Gusto** and **QuickBooks Time** handle U.S. federal and state labor laws well, but if you’re in Canada or the EU, you’ll need to manually verify their calculations. **Deputy** supports multi-country compliance but lacks built-in tools for union rules or collective bargaining agreements. I’ve seen two clients get hit with fines because their software didn’t account for local meal-break laws.

**Scalability is a myth for some tools.** **Homebase** works great for teams under 50 but buckles under the weight of 100+ employees. Their scheduling tool becomes sluggish, and bulk edits (like adjusting shift times for an entire department) require manual updates. **Clockify** scales better but lacks native payroll integration, so you’ll need a third-party tool like **Rippling** or **BambooHR** to bridge the gap.

**User adoption is harder than vendors admit.** Employees resist new tools, especially if they’re used to paper timesheets or a simple punch-clock system. **Deputy’s** kiosk mode is the most intuitive for non-desk workers, but their training materials are dense and jargon-heavy. **Homebase** offers the best onboarding experience with short video tutorials and in-app prompts, but their mobile app’s UI is so stripped-down that power users find it limiting.

## What the Vendor Won’t Tell You
**Migration is a nightmare.** Moving from **ADP Workforce Now** to **Deputy** took one of my clients *six weeks*—not the “one-click import” the sales team promised. **QuickBooks Time** claims to sync with **Paychex**, but the integration only pulls in employee data, not historical time records. If you’re switching from a legacy system, budget for at least a month of parallel testing and double-check every payroll run.

**Community sentiment is mixed.** On **G2**, **Deputy** scores a 4.5 (as of June 2026), but dig into the reviews, and you’ll find recurring complaints about “unexpected billing” and “glitchy geofencing.” **Homebase** has a 4.6, with users praising its simplicity but criticizing the lack of advanced features. **Clockify’s** free tier gets rave reviews (4.7), but paid users grumble about the “nickel-and-diming” for add-ons like SSO and audit logs.

**There’s no one-size-fits-all.** If you’re a **retail chain with high turnover**, **Homebase’s** flat-rate pricing and easy onboarding are worth the trade-offs. If you’re a **construction firm with field crews**, **Deputy’s** geofencing and job costing justify the higher cost. If you’re a **small business already using QuickBooks**, **QuickBooks Time** is the path of least resistance—just brace for the fees.

Here’s the truth: the “best” time and attendance software is the one your team will actually use. Test the mobile app with your least tech-savvy employee. Run a payroll cycle in parallel with your old system. And for the love of spreadsheets, read the fine print on pricing. If you do that, you’ll avoid the tools that look great in a demo but collapse under real-world use.
