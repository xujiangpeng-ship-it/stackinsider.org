Procore’s “unlimited users” pitch sounds good until you realize every subcontractor, owner, or architect counts against your monthly active user limit. Each extra seat costs $500 per month if you exceed the cap. Teams scaling from 50 to 200 users have reported budget overruns of 30-40% after the first year, according to a 2025 G2 survey.

If you manage a mid-sized GC with 3-5 concurrent projects, this pricing model can feel like a bait-and-switch. The tools that work best aren’t the ones with the flashiest dashboards. They’re the ones that don’t penalize collaboration.

## What makes each tool different

Procore, Autodesk Construction Cloud (ACC), and Buildertrend lead the market, but each solves different problems.

Procore handles document control well. RFIs, submittals, and transmittals stay in one place. The mobile app lets supers attach photos directly to punch items. Field teams used to juggle spreadsheets and email chains see this as a daily improvement. The downside is the learning curve. New users need 2-3 weeks of training to get comfortable. The interface feels cluttered if you don’t use every module.

Autodesk Construction Cloud works best for teams already using Autodesk products. If you rely on Revit or BIM 360, ACC’s model coordination tools fit naturally. Clash detection and 3D model overlays update in real time, reducing rework. For projects outside design-heavy work, the value drops. Smaller contractors say ACC feels like overkill for basic scheduling and budget tracking.

Buildertrend suits residential and light commercial builders. The estimating tool connects with QuickBooks. The client portal lets homeowners approve change orders without endless emails. For teams under 20, it’s easier to adopt than Procore or ACC. It lacks deep BIM or model coordination features, so it won’t work for complex commercial projects.

## The drawbacks

No tool is perfect. Some limitations cause bigger problems than others.

Procore’s mobile app isn’t offline-first. Spotty cell service means losing access to drawings and punch lists until you reconnect. Remote projects often require paper backups.

ACC’s pricing isn’t transparent. Autodesk’s website says “contact sales” for all tiers. Leaked internal docs from 2024 show enterprise plans start at $1,500 per month for 10 users. Add-ons like model coordination cost another $500. Smaller teams report sticker shock when they realize they’re paying for unused features.

Buildertrend’s scheduling tool is basic. It works for linear projects but struggles with multiple phases and overlapping trades. Users often pair it with Primavera or Excel.

## The gaps

Most tools miss true offline functionality. Procore markets itself as “field-ready” but requires a live connection for core features. Remote teams or large sites with dead zones often print drawings or use third-party apps like PlanGrid for offline access.

Subcontractor adoption is another issue. Procore and ACC assume subs will log in daily, but many prefer email or text. Buildertrend’s client portal helps but isn’t a complete solution. Some GCs pay for duplicate licenses to keep subs engaged.

Migration takes time. Moving from spreadsheets or legacy tools like Sage 300 to Procore or ACC isn’t just about data transfer. Teams report 2-3 months of reduced productivity during the transition, even with training. Smaller builders stick with Buildertrend or eSUB because onboarding is faster.

## What users say

Reddit and trade forums highlight frustrations vendors don’t mention. On r/Construction, users call out Procore’s add-on costs. One comment from May 2026 said, “We got sold on ‘unlimited users,’ but our owner rep logging in once a month counts as an active seat. That’s $500 we didn’t budget for.”

ACC users like the BIM tools but criticize Autodesk’s support. A 2025 G2 review noted, “The software is powerful, but good luck getting someone on the phone when you’re mid-project and something breaks.”

Buildertrend’s community is smaller but loyal. The biggest complaint is the mobile app crashing during large photo uploads. Users recommend batching photos to avoid losing work.

## Which tool fits your team

Mid-large commercial GCs with budgets and tech-savvy teams should consider Procore. Just plan for overages and training.

Design-build firms or BIM-heavy teams will find value in Autodesk ACC. Negotiate pricing and ask for a dedicated support rep.

Residential builders or small commercial contractors should look at Buildertrend or eSUB. They save money and avoid unnecessary features. If projects are complex, pair Buildertrend with a dedicated scheduling tool.

Subcontractors need eSUB. It’s cheaper than Procore and ACC and aligns with trade-specific workflows.

The best tool isn’t about features. It’s about what your team will use. Procore’s document control is strong, but if your supers won’t adopt it, a simpler option works better. Start with a 30-day trial. Invite a few subs to test collaboration features. See who complains the least. That’s how you’ll find the right fit.
