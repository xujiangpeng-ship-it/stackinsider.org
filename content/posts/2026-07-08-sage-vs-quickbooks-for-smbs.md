The first time I migrated a 15-person manufacturing shop from spreadsheets to actual accounting software, the owner nearly cried when QuickBooks wouldn’t let him run a job-cost report without upgrading. Sage had the feature, but the interface looked like it was built in 2005. That’s the tension here: you’re choosing between a tool that does what you need and one that doesn’t make you hate using it.

## What you’ll actually pay

QuickBooks Online starts at $30/month for the Simple Start plan. That covers one user, basic invoicing, and expense tracking. The next tier, Essentials, jumps to $60/month for three users and bill management. For inventory or job costing, you’re looking at $90/month (Plus) or $200/month (Advanced) for up to 25 users. Payroll adds another $50–$80/month plus $5–$8 per employee.

Sage Accounting starts at $10/month for the entry-level plan, which is bare-bones: one user, invoicing, and bank feeds. The mid-tier plan is $25/month for unlimited users, cash flow forecasting, and purchase invoices. For inventory or project tracking, you’ll need Sage 50cloud, which starts at $56/month for one user and scales to $160/month for five users. Payroll is an add-on, typically $30–$50/month plus $4–$6 per employee.

Both vendors charge extra for live bank feeds if you exceed a certain number of transactions per month. QuickBooks caps at 5,000 transactions per year on the Plus plan; Sage doesn’t publish a limit but will throttle feeds if you hit 10,000+ monthly. If you’re reconciling daily, budget an extra $10–$20/month for either.

## Features that actually matter

QuickBooks wins on usability. The dashboard loads fast, the mobile app lets you snap receipts and reconcile on the go, and the reporting is customizable without needing a manual. If you’ve ever used a modern web app, you’ll figure it out in a day. The trade-off: some features feel simplified. Job costing exists, but it’s not as granular as Sage’s. You can track expenses by project, but you can’t allocate overhead or run WIP reports without exporting to Excel.

Sage does more out of the box for inventory and manufacturing. You can set up assemblies, track serial numbers, and run detailed job-cost reports without add-ons. The downside: the interface is cluttered. The desktop version (Sage 50cloud) still uses a Windows 95-style menu system, and the web app is a stripped-down version of the desktop. If your team is used to spreadsheets, they’ll adapt. If they expect something like Shopify or Slack, they’ll complain.

Both integrate with payment processors, but QuickBooks Payments is built in. Sage partners with third parties like Stripe and PayPal, which means extra fees and slower payouts. If you process a lot of credit cards, QuickBooks will save you 0.5–1% per transaction.

## Where they fall short

QuickBooks Online has a hard limit of 5,000 transactions per year on the Plus plan. If you’re reconciling daily or have high-volume sales, you’ll hit this fast. Upgrading to Advanced ($200/month) removes the limit, but that’s a steep jump for a small team. Sage doesn’t have a published limit, but the bank feed slows down if you exceed 10,000 transactions per month. Neither tool is great for high-volume accounting.

Sage’s mobile app is read-only. You can view invoices and check balances, but you can’t create or edit anything. QuickBooks’ mobile app is full-featured: you can send invoices, reconcile transactions, and even run payroll from your phone. If your team is remote or on-site, this is a dealbreaker.

Both tools have weak project management. QuickBooks has a basic “Projects” feature, but it’s just a way to group transactions. Sage has a separate “Sage Project Accounting” module, but it’s an add-on that costs extra and doesn’t integrate smoothly with the main accounting software. If you need Gantt charts or time tracking, you’ll need a separate tool like Asana or Harvest.

## Who should pick what

QuickBooks is the better choice if:
- Your team is under 10 people.
- You need mobile access and modern usability.
- You process payments or invoices on the go.
- You don’t need deep inventory or manufacturing features.

Sage is the better choice if:
- You have 10–50 employees.
- You need detailed job costing or inventory tracking.
- Your team is comfortable with older software.
- You’re in manufacturing, construction, or wholesale.

If you’re still unsure, try both. QuickBooks offers a 30-day free trial; Sage offers 30 days for Sage Accounting and 7 days for Sage 50cloud. Don’t just watch the demos—import your real data and run a few reports. The tool that lets you close the books faster is the one you should pick.
