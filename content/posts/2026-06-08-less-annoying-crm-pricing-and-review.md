Less Annoying CRM (LACRM) does one thing well: it strips away the bloat of enterprise CRMs and gives small teams a straightforward way to track contacts, deals, and tasks. But that simplicity comes with trade-offs—some expected, others less so. The pricing is easy to understand, but it’s not always the bargain it seems. And while the workflows are intuitive, they can feel rigid if your team’s needs grow beyond the basics.

## What You’ll Actually Pay

LACRM’s pricing is refreshingly transparent: **$15 per user per month, flat rate, no tiers, no surprises**. That’s the headline. But here’s the catch: if you need more than the basics, you’ll pay for it elsewhere.

- **No free tier**. Unlike HubSpot or Zoho, there’s no free plan to test the waters. You get a 30-day trial, but after that, it’s $15/user/month or nothing.
- **No volume discounts**. Whether you have 5 users or 50, the price per seat stays the same. For teams over 20, this can add up fast compared to competitors like Freshsales, which offers tiered pricing that gets cheaper per user as you scale.
- **No built-in email marketing or automation**. If you want to send bulk emails or set up drip campaigns, you’ll need to integrate with a third-party tool like Mailchimp or ActiveCampaign. That’s an extra $10–$30/month, depending on your list size.
- **No native telephony**. Want to log calls or use click-to-dial? You’ll need to integrate with a VoIP provider like Aircall or Kixie, adding another $25–$50/user/month.

As of June 2024, LACRM’s G2 rating sits at **4.8/5 from 500+ reviews**, with users praising its ease of use and customer support. But dig into the lower-rated reviews, and a pattern emerges: **teams feel nickel-and-dimed by the lack of built-in features**. One user on Reddit put it bluntly: *"It’s simple until you realize simple means missing."*

## Features That Actually Matter

LACRM’s strength is its no-frills approach. Here’s what works—and what doesn’t—when you’re using it day-to-day.

### What Sets It Apart
- **Contact management that doesn’t overcomplicate**. Adding a contact is as simple as filling out a form, and the interface doesn’t bury important fields under layers of tabs. For teams that just need a shared address book with notes and follow-up reminders, this is a breath of fresh air.
- **Task and calendar sync**. LACRM’s task system is basic but effective. You can assign tasks to team members, set due dates, and sync with Google Calendar or Outlook. It’s not a full project management tool, but for sales teams that live in their inboxes, it’s enough to keep things moving.
- **Customer support that responds**. LACRM’s support team is famously hands-on. They’ll hop on a call to walk you through setup, and their average response time is under an hour (per their [support page](https://www.lessannoyingcrm.com/support)). For small teams without an IT department, this is a lifesaver.

### Where It Falls Short
- **No custom fields for deals**. This is a big one. LACRM lets you track deals (or "opportunities," in their terminology), but you can’t add custom fields to them. If your sales process requires tracking things like "contract value" or "probability to close," you’re out of luck. You’ll have to hack it with notes or tags, which gets messy fast.
- **No API for custom integrations**. LACRM offers Zapier and native integrations with tools like Mailchimp and QuickBooks, but if you need a custom integration, you’re stuck. There’s no public API, so you can’t pull data into a BI tool or build a custom dashboard. This is a dealbreaker for teams that rely on data analytics.
- **Mobile app is read-only**. The mobile app lets you view contacts and tasks, but you can’t edit or add new records. For sales reps who need to update deals on the go, this is a major limitation. The workaround? Use the mobile browser version, which is clunky but functional.

## The Rough Edges

LACRM’s marketing sells it as the "CRM for people who hate CRMs," and for the most part, that’s true. But there are a few rough edges that the website doesn’t highlight.

- **Migration is manual**. If you’re switching from another CRM, there’s no automated import tool. You’ll need to export your data as a CSV and manually map fields. LACRM’s support team will help, but it’s still a time-consuming process. One user on G2 reported spending **10+ hours migrating 500 contacts** from HubSpot.
- **No duplicate detection**. LACRM doesn’t automatically flag duplicate contacts or companies. If your team is prone to typos or importing the same list multiple times, you’ll end up with a messy database. The workaround is to export your contacts periodically and use a tool like Excel to deduplicate.
- **Limited reporting**. LACRM’s reporting is basic: you can see open deals, tasks by user, and a few other pre-built reports. But there’s no way to create custom reports or save report templates. If you need to track metrics like "average deal size by lead source," you’ll have to export the data and crunch it in Excel or Google Sheets.

LACRM is the simplest option on this list, but it’s also the least flexible. If your team needs **custom fields, automation, or advanced reporting**, you’ll outgrow it quickly. HubSpot CRM is the closest alternative in terms of ease of use, and it’s free for up to 1 million contacts. Freshsales is a better fit for teams that need **built-in telephony and AI-powered lead scoring**.

## Who It’s For (And Who Should Look Elsewhere)

LACRM is a great fit for **solopreneurs, small service businesses, and teams of 1–10 people** who need a shared contact database with basic task management. If you’re a freelancer, consultant, or small agency that just needs to track leads and follow-ups, it’s hard to beat the simplicity.

But if you’re a **growing sales team, a business with complex deal tracking needs, or a company that relies on data analytics**, LACRM will feel limiting. The lack of custom fields, API access, and advanced reporting will frustrate you sooner rather than later. In those cases, Freshsales or Pipedrive are better choices—even if they come with a steeper learning curve.

The bottom line? LACRM delivers on its promise of being "less annoying," but that simplicity comes at a cost. If you’re okay with its limitations, it’s a solid tool. If not, you’ll be shopping for a replacement within a year.
