Small business owners often assume PEOs (Professional Employer Organizations) will save them money on benefits and HR headaches. Then they get the invoice.

Most PEOs charge a per-employee fee *plus* a percentage of payroll—typically 2-8%. For a 10-person team with $500k in annual payroll, that’s $10k–$40k *on top* of base costs. And if you’re growing fast, those percentages can quietly eat into your margins. The real kicker? Some PEOs lock you into multi-year contracts with automatic renewal clauses buried in the fine print.

Let’s cut through the sales pitches and look at what actually works—and where these services fall short.

## What You’ll Actually Pay

PEO pricing isn’t transparent. Most vendors won’t give you a quote without a sales call, and even then, the numbers shift based on your industry, headcount, and benefits package. Here’s what you can expect:

- **Justworks**: Starts at $59/month per employee for their basic plan, but jumps to $99/month if you want their full suite (including 401(k) and medical benefits). No percentage-of-payroll fee, which is rare.
- **Insperity**: Charges a base fee *plus* 3-6% of payroll. Their G2 rating (4.2 as of June 2026) reflects mixed reviews on pricing transparency—some users report unexpected fees for compliance filings.
- **ADP TotalSource**: Their website lists "custom pricing," but Reddit threads and Glassdoor reviews suggest a 4-8% payroll fee, with additional costs for benefits administration. One user on r/smallbusiness reported a $1,200 "implementation fee" that wasn’t disclosed upfront.
- **Trinet**: Also uses a percentage model (3-7% of payroll), but their G2 score (4.0) dips lower due to complaints about customer service response times. Their "PEO Lite" plan excludes COBRA administration, which can be a nasty surprise if you have part-time employees.

If you’re a team of 5-20, Justworks is the only major PEO that avoids percentage-based fees entirely. For larger teams (50+), Insperity and ADP offer more customization—but you’ll pay for it.

## Where It Shines (and Where It Doesn’t)

### The Good: What PEOs Actually Solve
1. **Benefits at scale**: PEOs pool employees across clients to negotiate better rates on health insurance, 401(k) plans, and even commuter benefits. A 10-person startup can offer the same Blue Cross Blue Shield plan as a 500-person company—without the underwriting hassle.
2. **Compliance offloading**: PEOs handle payroll taxes, workers’ comp audits, and even I-9 verifications. ADP TotalSource, for example, includes built-in alerts for state-specific labor law changes (like California’s new paid sick leave updates in 2026). This alone can justify the cost for teams without an in-house HR person.
3. **HR tools you’d otherwise build yourself**: Most PEOs include an employee portal for onboarding, time tracking, and performance reviews. Justworks’ mobile app lets employees submit PTO requests or update direct deposit info without bugging HR—which is a bigger deal than it sounds.

### The Rough Edges: What They Don’t Tell You
- **Integration gaps**: PEOs rarely play nice with other software. ADP TotalSource, for instance, doesn’t sync with QuickBooks Online—you’ll need to export CSV files manually. Insperity’s API is locked behind an enterprise plan, so smaller teams are stuck with clunky workarounds.
- **Customer service black holes**: Trinet’s G2 reviews frequently mention slow response times (some users report 48+ hours for payroll issues). One Reddit user complained that their payroll specialist changed three times in six months, forcing them to re-explain their setup repeatedly.
- **Benefits aren’t always better**: PEOs market "Fortune 500-level benefits," but the plans can be restrictive. Some exclude telehealth or have narrow provider networks. Always compare the PEO’s offerings to what you could get directly from a broker like Gusto or Rippling.
- **Contract traps**: Most PEOs require 12-24 month commitments. ADP’s contract includes a 90-day notice period for cancellation—miss it, and you’re auto-renewed. Always negotiate this clause upfront.

## The Hidden Cost: Migration Effort
Switching to a PEO isn’t just about signing a contract. You’ll need to:
- Reclassify employees under the PEO’s EIN (which can trigger new workers’ comp audits).
- Migrate payroll data (expect 2-4 weeks of parallel testing to avoid errors).
- Retrain your team on the PEO’s portal (Justworks’ onboarding is smoother than ADP’s, which still uses a desktop-only interface for some tasks).

One CFO I worked with estimated 40 hours of internal work to migrate 30 employees to Insperity—mostly spent cleaning up payroll data and reconciling tax filings.

## Who Should (and Shouldn’t) Use a PEO
**Go for it if:**
- You’re a team of 5-200 without an HR department.
- You want to offer competitive benefits without hiring a full-time benefits manager.
- Compliance (like ACA reporting or state-specific labor laws) keeps you up at night.

**Avoid if:**
- You’re a solopreneur or micro-business (under 5 employees). Tools like Gusto or Rippling are cheaper and more flexible.
- You need deep integrations with niche software (e.g., construction payroll or nonprofit grant tracking). PEOs prioritize breadth over depth.
- You’re in a high-turnover industry (like retail or hospitality). The per-employee fees add up fast when you’re constantly onboarding new hires.

## The Bottom Line
PEOs aren’t a magic bullet, but for the right team, they’re worth the cost. Justworks is the safest bet for small businesses—transparent pricing, no percentage fees, and a user-friendly interface. If you’re larger (50+ employees) and need customization, Insperity or ADP TotalSource can work, but push back hard on contract terms and hidden fees.

Before signing, run a cost comparison: tally your current HR spend (salaries, benefits broker fees, payroll software, etc.) and compare it to the PEO’s quote. If the savings are less than 15%, keep shopping.
