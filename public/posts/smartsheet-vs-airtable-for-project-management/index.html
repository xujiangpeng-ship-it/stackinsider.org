Smartsheet vs Airtable for Project Management: Which Actually Fits Your Team?

Smartsheet and Airtable both promise project management flexibility, but one forces you into spreadsheets while the other drowns you in setup. Here’s which to pick—and when to walk away.

Jun 7, 2026 5 minutes read

Smartsheet’s free tier doesn’t include Gantt charts. That’s the first thing most teams discover after signing up, and it’s a dealbreaker for anyone managing timelines. Airtable’s free plan, by contrast, lets you build basic project trackers—but you’ll hit the 1,000-record limit faster than you expect if you’re logging tasks, comments, and attachments. Neither tool is truly free for real work, and both vendors know it.

What You’ll Actually Pay

Smartsheet’s pricing starts at $7 per user/month (billed annually), but the Pro plan—the first tier with Gantt charts—costs $25 per user/month. Airtable’s equivalent, the Plus plan, is $20 per user/month. Both require annual contracts to avoid a 20% surcharge, a detail buried in the FAQs. For a team of 10, that’s $3,000 vs. $2,400 per year, but the real cost comes later: Smartsheet’s admin controls and reporting tools are locked behind the Business plan ($59/user/month), while Airtable’s advanced features (like sync and extensions) kick in at $45/user/month.

Smartsheet’s Gantt charts are native and reliable, but Airtable’s workaround—using the Gantt extension—requires manual setup and breaks if you rename fields. If timelines are critical, Smartsheet wins. If you need flexibility and don’t mind extra clicks, Airtable’s approach lets you mix project data with other workflows (like CRM or inventory) in the same base.

Where It Shines (and Where It Doesn’t)

Smartsheet looks like Excel but behaves like a project tool. That’s its biggest strength and its biggest weakness. Teams already using spreadsheets for tracking will adapt quickly, but the interface feels dated—like a 2010-era web app with extra steps. The mobile app is functional but clunky; offline mode works, but syncing changes can take minutes if you’re on a slow connection.

Airtable, on the other hand, feels modern and flexible. You can build a project tracker, a content calendar, and a client database in the same workspace. The downside? That flexibility comes with setup overhead. A simple task list in Airtable requires more configuration than Smartsheet’s pre-built templates, and the learning curve is steeper for non-technical users. One real-world example: a marketing team I worked with spent two weeks tweaking an Airtable base for campaign tracking, only to abandon it because the view filters were too complex for their contractors to use.

Both tools support automations, but Airtable’s are more powerful—if you’re willing to write formulas. Smartsheet’s automations are simpler (e.g., “send an email when status changes”), but they’re limited to 250 runs/month on the Pro plan. Airtable’s Plus plan allows 5,000 runs, which is enough for most teams, but the Pro plan’s 50,000 runs are overkill unless you’re automating at scale.

The Rough Edges

Smartsheet’s reporting is a mess. The “report builder” is essentially a filtered grid view, and exporting to PDF or Excel often breaks formatting. Airtable’s reporting is even worse—it doesn’t exist. You’ll need to use a third-party tool like Superchart or export data to Google Sheets for visualizations.

Integration friction is another pain point. Smartsheet integrates with Microsoft 365 and Google Workspace, but the connections are shallow. For example, you can attach a Google Drive file to a task, but you can’t edit it inline—you have to open a new tab. Airtable’s integrations are deeper (e.g., you can embed a Google Doc directly in a record), but the sync can be slow. A client once lost a day’s work because Airtable’s Google Drive sync failed silently, and the changes didn’t save.

Community sentiment leans toward frustration with both tools. On G2, Smartsheet’s rating is 4.4/5 (as of June 2026), but users consistently complain about the lack of native time tracking and the clunky mobile app. Airtable’s rating is 4.6/5, but the top complaints are about the steep learning curve and the cost of scaling. One Reddit user put it bluntly: “Airtable is like LEGO—powerful if you have the time to build, but most teams just need a pre-assembled set.”

What Sets It Apart

Smartsheet’s standout feature is its resource management tool. You can assign team members to tasks, track their workload, and even see a heatmap of overbooked resources. It’s not perfect—it doesn’t account for meetings or ad-hoc work—but it’s better than Airtable’s manual approach. Airtable’s strength is its extensibility. With extensions like Page Designer, you can generate PDFs from records (e.g., invoices, contracts), something Smartsheet can’t do natively.

For teams that need to mix project management with other workflows (e.g., a software team tracking bugs alongside sprints), Airtable’s flexibility is unmatched. But if you’re managing pure project timelines and don’t want to spend weeks configuring views, Smartsheet’s structured approach will save you time.

Who Should Use What

Pick Smartsheet if:

  • Your team lives in spreadsheets and needs Gantt charts without extra setup.
  • You’re managing projects with clear timelines and dependencies (e.g., construction, event planning).
  • You need basic resource management and don’t want to pay for a separate tool.

Pick Airtable if:

  • Your projects involve multiple workflows (e.g., marketing campaigns with asset tracking, or product development with bug reports).
  • You’re willing to invest time in setup for long-term flexibility.
  • You need to generate documents or reports from your project data.

Avoid both if:

  • You need robust time tracking (try ClickUp or Harvest instead).
  • Your team is resistant to change—both tools require training.
  • You’re on a tight budget. The “free” plans are teases, and the paid tiers add up quickly.

If you’re still unsure, run a pilot with both tools. Smartsheet’s templates will get you up and running in an hour; Airtable’s will take a week. That alone might make the decision for you.
