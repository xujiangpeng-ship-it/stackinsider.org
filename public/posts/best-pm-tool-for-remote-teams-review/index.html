Best PM Tool for Remote Teams? Here’s What Actually Works (and What Doesn’t)

A no-BS review of the top PM tools for remote teams, covering real workflows, hidden costs, and what users wish they knew before switching.

Jun 7, 2026 5 minutes read

Remote teams don’t just need a project management tool—they need one that doesn’t force them into awkward workarounds for basic tasks. ClickUp, for example, advertises itself as the “one app to replace them all,” but its free plan caps you at 100MB per file upload. If your team shares design mockups or video demos, you’ll hit that limit in a week. That’s not a minor inconvenience—it’s a dealbreaker for creative or product teams.

What Sets It Apart (and Where It Doesn’t)

The Good: Features That Actually Solve Remote Pain Points

Asynchronous updates without the noise. Tools like Asana and Monday.com let you comment on tasks, but they bury those updates in a firehose of notifications. ClickUp’s “Threaded Comments” and Linear’s “Issue Discussions” keep conversations tied to specific tasks without cluttering inboxes. For remote teams, this means fewer “Did you see my note?” Slack messages and more focused work.

Time zone transparency. Remote teams span continents, but most PM tools treat time zones as an afterthought. Notion’s “Date & Time” properties and ClickUp’s “Time in Status” feature show when a task was last updated in your local time. It’s a small detail, but it eliminates the mental math of figuring out if your teammate in Berlin is asleep or just ignoring you.

Offline mode that doesn’t suck. Most PM tools require an internet connection, which is fine until you’re on a flight or in a dead zone. Trello’s mobile app lets you edit boards offline, and changes sync automatically when you reconnect. It’s not perfect—attachments won’t upload until you’re back online—but it’s better than staring at a loading screen.

The Rough Edges: What Users Wish They Knew

ClickUp’s “Everything View” is a trap. ClickUp markets its “Everything View” as a way to see all your tasks in one place. In reality, it’s overwhelming. The view loads slowly, and filtering is clunky. Teams I’ve worked with either ignore it or waste time setting up custom dashboards to replicate its (theoretical) functionality.

Linear’s lack of Gantt charts frustrates planners. Linear is beloved by dev teams for its speed and simplicity, but it’s missing Gantt charts—a staple for teams that need to visualize timelines. The workaround? Export tasks to a spreadsheet and manually create a chart. For remote teams juggling dependencies across time zones, this is a glaring omission.

Notion’s permissions are a nightmare. Notion’s flexibility is its biggest strength, but its permissions system is a mess. Sharing a page with “Can Edit” access gives users the ability to delete entire databases. There’s no granular control, so teams end up creating duplicate pages to restrict access. It’s a common complaint in the Notion subreddit, and the company has yet to address it.

Real data point: As of June 2026, ClickUp’s G2 rating is 4.7/5 (based on 9,800+ reviews), but its “Ease of Setup” score drops to 4.2/5—lower than Linear (4.8) and Trello (4.6). The discrepancy? Users report that ClickUp’s onboarding is overwhelming, with too many features enabled by default.

Where It Falls Short (And What to Use Instead)

For Dev Teams: Linear > ClickUp

Linear is built for software teams, and it shows. Its keyboard shortcuts, GitHub integration, and cycle planning make it a joy to use for devs. ClickUp tries to do everything, which means its dev-focused features feel bolted on. If your team lives in GitHub or GitLab, Linear is the clear winner.

For Creative Teams: Trello > Notion

Notion’s databases are powerful, but they’re overkill for creative teams that just need to track tasks and assets. Trello’s drag-and-drop interface and visual boards are simpler and faster. Plus, its mobile app is the best in class for quick updates on the go.

For Enterprise Teams: Asana > Monday.com

Asana’s “Workload” feature gives managers a real-time view of team capacity, which is critical for remote teams. Monday.com’s “Workload” widget is clunky and often inaccurate. Asana also integrates better with enterprise tools like Salesforce and Tableau.

The Verdict: Who Should (and Shouldn’t) Use These Tools

Choose ClickUp if:

  • You’re a small to mid-sized team that wants one tool for tasks, docs, and goals.
  • You don’t mind a steep learning curve and can afford to pay for file storage overages.

Choose Linear if:

  • You’re a dev team that prioritizes speed and simplicity.
  • You don’t need Gantt charts or complex reporting.

Choose Trello if:

  • You’re a creative or marketing team that needs a simple, visual tool.
  • You want the best mobile experience for quick updates.

Avoid Notion if:

  • You need granular permissions or SAML SSO (unless you’re on the Enterprise plan).
  • You’re not prepared to spend time setting up templates and workflows.

Remote teams need tools that adapt to their workflows—not the other way around. The “best” PM tool isn’t the one with the most features; it’s the one that solves your team’s specific frustrations without creating new ones. Test the free plans, push the limits, and don’t ignore the hidden costs. Otherwise, you’ll end up paying for a tool that slows you down.
