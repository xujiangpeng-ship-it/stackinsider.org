Best PM for Creative Agencies: The Tools That Actually Work (and the Ones That Don’t)

A no-BS review of the best project management tools for creative agencies—what works, what frustrates, and where they fall short.

Jun 7, 2026 7 minutes read

Creative agencies need project management software that doesn’t just track tasks—it keeps clients happy, deadlines realistic, and teams from burning out. Most tools claim to do this, but only a few actually nail the workflows agencies rely on: visual timelines, client-friendly reporting, and flexible approvals. The rest either drown you in complexity or force you to hack together workarounds.

Here’s the catch: the “best” tool depends entirely on whether you prioritize speed, customization, or client transparency. And if you’re not careful, you’ll end up paying for features you’ll never use—or worse, a tool that slows your team down.

What Creative Agencies Actually Need (Spoiler: Most Tools Miss the Mark)

Most PM software is built for software teams or generic business ops. Creative agencies? They need three things that don’t always align with mainstream tools:

  1. Visual project planning – Gantt charts are great, but creative teams live in Kanban or timeline views. They need to see dependencies at a glance, not dig through nested subtasks.
  2. Client-friendly collaboration – Agencies need a way to loop in clients without exposing internal chaos. Most tools either hide everything behind permissions or dump clients into a cluttered interface.
  3. Flexible approvals – A “done” task in a creative agency often means “approved by the client,” not just “moved to the next column.” Few tools handle this well without manual workarounds.

If a tool doesn’t solve at least two of these, it’s not worth your time.

What Sets Them Apart (For Better or Worse)

ClickUp: The Swiss Army Knife (If You Can Handle the Complexity)

ClickUp is the only tool here that lets you build a true creative workflow without duct-taping integrations. Need a custom status for “Client Review (Round 2)”? Done. Want to auto-assign tasks based on client feedback? Also done.

But here’s the rub: ClickUp’s flexibility is a double-edged sword. Small agencies (under 10 people) often waste hours tweaking settings instead of shipping work. And while the mobile app is decent, offline mode is still missing—a dealbreaker for teams on the go.

Real-world win: One agency I worked with cut their project setup time in half by using ClickUp’s recurring task templates for client onboarding. No more forgetting to schedule the kickoff call.

Hidden gotcha: ClickUp’s “Unlimited” plan isn’t actually unlimited. If your team exceeds 100,000 tasks or 10,000 automation runs per month, you’ll get throttled (source: ClickUp’s official pricing page). For most agencies, this won’t matter—but if you’re managing 50+ active projects, watch your usage.

Monday.com: The Client-Friendly Showcase

Monday.com shines when you need to impress clients. Its shareable dashboards let you give clients a clean, branded view of their project—no accidental peeks at internal notes. The timeline view is also one of the best for creative teams, with drag-and-drop rescheduling that actually works.

But Monday.com’s pricing is sneaky. The “Basic” plan ($8/user/month) lacks time tracking and automations—two features most agencies need. Upgrade to “Standard” ($10/user/month), and you’re still capped at 250 automations/month. For a team of 10, that’s gone in a week.

Real-world win: A mid-sized agency used Monday.com’s workload view to spot a designer consistently overbooked. They redistributed tasks, cutting overtime by 30% in a month.

Hidden gotcha: Monday.com’s file storage limits are aggressively low. The “Standard” plan only gives you 20GB total—not per user. If you’re handling high-res assets (PSDs, videos), you’ll hit that ceiling fast. (Source: Monday.com’s storage policy.)

Asana: The Simple Choice (If You Don’t Need Much)

Asana is the easiest tool to adopt. The interface is clean, the learning curve is gentle, and it’s great for agencies that just need to track deadlines and assign tasks.

But Asana’s simplicity comes at a cost. Approvals are clunky—you’ll need to use a third-party integration (like ApprovalMax) or hack together a custom field. And if you need time tracking, you’re stuck with another tool (like Harvest) or Asana’s native feature, which is barebones.

Real-world win: A small agency switched from Trello to Asana and cut their weekly check-in meetings from 60 minutes to 15. The Timeline view made it easy to spot bottlenecks before they became crises.

Hidden gotcha: Asana’s search functionality is weak. If you’re managing 50+ projects, finding a specific task can feel like digging through a haystack. The Advanced Search feature (available on the Business plan) helps, but it’s not intuitive.

Wrike: The Enterprise Workhorse (If You Can Afford the Learning Curve)

Wrike is the only tool here built for large agencies (50+ people) with complex approval chains. Its custom workflows and proofing tools (for design feedback) are best-in-class.

But Wrike’s onboarding is brutal. One agency I consulted with spent three weeks training their team on Wrike’s terminology (“spaces,” “folders,” “projects”). And while the mobile app is functional, it’s slow—expect lags when loading large projects.

Real-world win: A global agency used Wrike’s request forms to standardize client briefs. No more “Can you just tweak this?” emails—everything went through a structured form.

Hidden gotcha: Wrike’s API rate limits are strict. If you’re syncing data with other tools (like Slack or Salesforce), you’ll hit the limit fast. (Source: Wrike’s API documentation.)

Trello: The Quick Fix (If You’re Small and Simple)

Trello is the only tool here that’s actually fun to use. The drag-and-drop interface is perfect for small teams (under 5 people) who need to move fast.

But Trello’s simplicity is also its downfall. No timeline view means you’ll struggle to plan long-term projects. And no native time tracking forces you to use a Power-Up (like Toggl), which adds friction.

Real-world win: A freelance designer used Trello to manage 20+ client projects solo. The Butler automation (free on the Standard plan) auto-archived completed tasks, keeping her board clean.

Hidden gotcha: Trello’s free plan is generous, but the file upload limit is 10MB per attachment. If you’re sharing design files, you’ll need to upgrade or use Google Drive.

The Rough Edges No One Talks About

Integration Friction

Most tools claim to “integrate seamlessly” with Adobe Creative Cloud, Slack, or Google Drive. In reality:

  • ClickUp’s Adobe integration only works with Photoshop and Illustrator—not InDesign or Premiere Pro.
  • Monday.com’s Slack integration is one-way. You can get Slack notifications, but you can’t update tasks from Slack.
  • Wrike’s Google Drive integration is slow. Expect 5–10 second delays when opening large files.

Migration Nightmares

Switching tools is always painful, but some make it worse:

  • Asana to Monday.com is smooth (they have a built-in importer).
  • Trello to ClickUp is a mess. You’ll lose labels, due dates, and checklists unless you use a third-party tool like Trello2ClickUp.
  • Wrike to anything else is brutal. Wrike’s export options are limited, and most tools can’t import its custom fields.

Client Onboarding

Most tools treat clients as an afterthought. Here’s what agencies actually deal with:

  • ClickUp’s guest access is flexible, but clients often complain about the cluttered interface.
  • Monday.com’s client portals look great, but they’re read-only. Clients can’t comment or upload files.
  • Asana’s guest permissions are all-or-nothing. You can’t give a client access to just one project—they see everything.

Which Tool Should You Actually Use?

  • If you’re a small agency (1–10 people) and need simplicity: Trello or Asana. Trello for speed, Asana for a bit more structure.
  • If you’re mid-sized (10–50 people) and need client-friendly features: Monday.com. It’s the best balance of visual planning and client transparency.
  • If you’re large (50+ people) or need deep customization: ClickUp or Wrike. ClickUp for flexibility, Wrike for enterprise-grade approvals.
  • If you’re on a tight budget: Trello’s free plan or ClickUp’s $7/user/month tier. Avoid Monday.com’s “Basic” plan—it’s too limited.

Final takeaway: The “best” tool is the one your team will actually use. Start with a free trial, migrate one project, and see where the friction points are. If your team groans every time they open the app, it’s not the right fit—no matter how many “best of” lists it’s on.
