Resource Management Tools with Time Tracking: What Teams Actually Get (and What They Don’t)

Honest review of resource management tools with time tracking—pricing traps, real workflow wins, and where they fall short for teams.

Jun 7, 2026 4 minutes read

Here’s the first thing no vendor will tell you: most resource management tools with time tracking charge extra for the one feature you actually need. If your team tracks time against projects, expect to pay 20–50% more per user for “premium” time-tracking modules. Float, for example, lists its base plan at $6/user/month, but time tracking is only included in the $10/user/month tier—effectively a 66% price hike. That’s before you factor in the cost of training, which, according to a 2025 G2 survey, averages 3–5 hours per user for tools like Resource Guru and Runn.

What Sets It Apart (and What’s Just Noise)

The best tools don’t just log hours—they show you where your team’s capacity is leaking. Float’s live capacity heatmap, for instance, updates in real time and color-codes overbooked resources. That’s useful if you’re running a 20-person agency with overlapping deadlines. But if you’re a 5-person startup, it’s overkill. The heatmap is only available on the $10/user/month plan, and even then, it doesn’t sync with QuickBooks for payroll—you’ll need a Zapier workaround that adds latency and another $20/month to your stack.

Runn, on the other hand, handles capacity planning and time tracking in one view, but its reporting is clunky. The “Project Profitability” dashboard requires manual tagging of billable vs. non-billable hours, and there’s no bulk edit. If you forget to tag a week’s worth of time, you’re stuck clicking through individual entries. That’s a dealbreaker for teams that bill by the hour.

The One Feature That Saves Time (If You Use It Right)

Most tools tout “automated time tracking,” but the real value is in the exceptions. Resource Guru’s “conflict alerts” pop up when someone is double-booked, and you can resolve it with a drag-and-drop. That’s saved my clients 2–3 hours per week in back-and-forth emails. But here’s the catch: the alerts only trigger if you’ve set up project phases and milestones. Skip that step, and the tool just becomes a glorified calendar.

The Rough Edges

Integration Friction You Won’t See on the Website

Float integrates with Slack, but the sync is one-way. Time entries logged in Float don’t update Slack statuses, so your team still gets “Are you free?” messages while they’re heads-down. Runn’s Jira integration is read-only—you can’t push time entries back into Jira, which defeats the purpose for dev teams. Resource Guru’s API is rate-limited to 100 requests per minute, a bottleneck for teams pulling data into BI tools like Power BI or Tableau.

Mobile Apps Are an Afterthought

Resource Guru’s mobile app lacks offline mode, and the time tracker crashes if you switch between Wi-Fi and cellular. Float’s app is more stable but only lets you log time—no capacity planning or conflict alerts. If your team is remote or field-based, you’ll need to budget for a separate time-tracking tool like Toggl Track, which adds $9/user/month to your costs.

The Hidden Cost of “Unlimited” Projects

Most tools advertise “unlimited projects,” but performance degrades after 500 active projects. Runn’s UI slows to a crawl, and Float’s heatmap becomes unreadable. If you’re a mid-sized consultancy juggling 300+ clients, you’ll hit this limit fast. The workaround? Archive old projects, but that breaks historical reporting unless you export data manually—a tedious process that takes 1–2 hours per month.

Where It Falls Short (And What to Use Instead)

If your team is under 10 people, Float’s $10/user/month plan is overkill. Toggl Plan ($9/user/month) handles time tracking and basic capacity planning without the bloat. For dev teams, Jira + Tempo Timesheets ($10/user/month) is a better fit—it syncs bidirectionally and supports Agile workflows.

For mid-sized agencies (20–50 people), Runn’s $10/user/month plan is the sweet spot, but only if you can live with its reporting quirks. If you need deep financial reporting, Harvest + Forecast is worth the $18/user/month—just be prepared for a steeper learning curve.

The Verdict

If you’re a small team that just needs to log hours and avoid double-booking, Resource Guru’s $4.16/user/month plan is the best value. But if you’re scaling and need real-time capacity insights, Float’s $10/user/month plan is worth the premium—just budget for the extra cost of Zapier or a custom integration.

The real question isn’t which tool is “best.” It’s which tool matches your team’s workflow without forcing you to change it. Pick the one that solves your biggest frustration today, not the one with the most features. You’ll outgrow it eventually, but by then, you’ll know exactly what you need next.
