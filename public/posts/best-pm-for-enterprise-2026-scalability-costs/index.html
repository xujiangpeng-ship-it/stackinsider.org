Best PM for Enterprise in 2026: What You’re Not Being Told About Scalability and Costs

A no-nonsense review of enterprise PM tools in 2026, revealing hidden costs, scalability limits, and real user frustrations.

Jun 7, 2026 5 minutes read

Enterprise project management tools promise seamless collaboration, but most teams hit a wall when scaling beyond 500 users. The sticker price on the vendor’s website rarely includes the mandatory “Enterprise Success Package” that adds 20–30% to annual costs—something you won’t discover until the final contract review.

What You’ll Actually Pay

Most enterprise PM tools advertise a per-user price, but the real cost emerges in add-ons. Asana’s Enterprise+ tier, for example, starts at $30/user/month, but enabling advanced security controls (like SCIM provisioning or custom SAML) requires an additional $5/user/month—bringing the total to $35. That’s before factoring in the $10,000–$25,000 one-time implementation fee for teams over 1,000 users, a detail buried in their official pricing FAQ under “Enterprise Deployment Services.”

Smartsheet and Monday.com follow a similar model. Smartsheet’s Enterprise plan is $25/user/month, but enabling premium support (24/7 phone access) bumps it to $35. Monday.com’s Enterprise tier is $24/user/month, but their “Advanced Reporting” add-on—critical for large teams—costs another $8/user/month. These aren’t optional for enterprises; they’re table stakes.

Where It Shines (and Where It Doesn’t)

The Good: Features That Scale

1. Workload Balancing Tools like ClickUp and Wrike offer workload views that actually work for enterprise teams. Unlike Jira’s basic capacity planning, these let you set custom workload thresholds by role (e.g., “Max 5 tasks/day for UX designers”) and auto-rebalance when someone hits 80% capacity. For a 200-person product team, this cuts weekly planning meetings from 2 hours to 20 minutes.

2. Cross-Departmental Dependencies Enterprise projects fail when finance, legal, and engineering operate in silos. Microsoft Project’s “Master Project” feature lets you link sub-projects across departments while maintaining individual timelines. A G2 review from May 2026 noted: “We cut cross-team dependency delays by 40% after migrating from Asana, which still lacks true multi-project linking.”

3. Audit Trails for Compliance For regulated industries (healthcare, finance), audit logs aren’t optional. Smartsheet’s “Activity Log” tracks every change—who edited a cell, when, and from which IP address—with 10-year retention. This is a lifesaver during SOC 2 audits, where most tools only retain logs for 90 days.

The Bad: What Users Complain About

1. Mobile Apps Are an Afterthought Enterprise users are often in the field, but most PM tools treat mobile as a checkbox. Jira’s mobile app, for instance, lacks offline mode and crashes when loading sprints with over 50 issues. A Reddit thread from March 2026 called it “a glorified to-do list with none of the power of the desktop version.”

2. Integration Friction Even tools with “1,000+ integrations” have gaps. Monday.com’s Salesforce integration, for example, syncs contacts but not custom objects—a dealbreaker for sales ops teams. Their official documentation admits this limitation, but it’s not mentioned on their marketing pages.

3. Reporting Is Either Too Basic or Too Complex Enterprise teams need both high-level dashboards and granular data. Most tools fail at one or the other. Asana’s reporting is simple but lacks custom SQL queries, while Microsoft Project’s reporting is so complex that teams often export data to Power BI—adding another tool to the stack.

The Rough Edges

1. Migration Is a Nightmare Moving from one PM tool to another is painful, but enterprise migrations are worse. A 2025 survey by Gartner found that 68% of enterprise teams underestimated migration effort, with the average project taking 4–6 months longer than planned. The biggest culprit? Custom fields and automation rules that don’t map cleanly between tools. Jira to ClickUp migrations, for example, often lose issue dependencies because Jira’s “blocks” relationship doesn’t exist in ClickUp.

2. Permission Structures Are Overly Complex Enterprise tools add layers of permissions (e.g., “Project Admin,” “Workspace Admin,” “Org Admin”), but most teams end up with either too much or too little access. A common complaint on TrustRadius is that Smartsheet’s permission model forces teams to either grant full edit access or none at all—no middle ground for “view-only with comments.”

3. AI Features Are Gimmicky Most PM tools now offer AI-powered task suggestions or auto-generated reports, but enterprise teams find them unreliable. A 2026 G2 review of Wrike’s AI noted: “It suggested assigning a task to a developer who left the company six months ago. The data isn’t fresh enough to be useful.”

Who Should (and Shouldn’t) Use It

Go with Asana if:

  • Your team is under 1,000 users and values simplicity over granular controls.
  • You’re in marketing or creative services and need visual project timelines.

Avoid Asana if:

  • You’re in a regulated industry (HIPAA, SOC 2) and need long-term audit logs.
  • Your workflows rely on complex dependencies across 50+ projects.

Go with Microsoft Project if:

  • You’re already deep in the Microsoft ecosystem (Azure, Power BI, Teams).
  • Your projects involve heavy resource allocation (e.g., construction, engineering).

Avoid Microsoft Project if:

  • Your team uses Macs or Linux—desktop app performance is noticeably worse than on Windows.
  • You need a tool that non-technical teams (HR, finance) can adopt without training.

Go with Smartsheet if:

  • Compliance is a top priority (audit logs, data retention).
  • You need a balance between spreadsheets and project management.

Avoid Smartsheet if:

  • Your team lives in mobile apps—Smartsheet’s mobile experience is clunky.
  • You rely on deep integrations with tools like Salesforce or HubSpot.

For most enterprise teams, the “best” PM tool isn’t about features—it’s about which limitations you can live with. If you’re in a regulated industry, Smartsheet’s audit trails might be worth the mobile trade-offs. If you’re a product team, ClickUp’s workload balancing could save you hours per week. But if you’re already using Microsoft 365, Project might be the path of least resistance, even if it’s not the most elegant solution. The key is to pilot with your most complex workflow first, not the simplest.
