<h1 class="article-title"> Best Budgeting and Forecasting Software: What Teams Actually Use (and Why) </h1> <h2 class="article-subtitle"> Honest review of top budgeting tools—pricing traps, real workflow wins, and where each falls short for finance teams. </h2>
Apr 24, 2026 6 minutes read

Jirav’s “Starter” plan is advertised at $500/month, but that’s only for 5 users. Add a sixth, and you’re bumped to the $1,000/month “Growth” tier—no prorated discount. That’s a 100% jump for one extra seat, and it’s buried in the pricing FAQ, not the splash page. If you’re a 10-person finance team, you’re already looking at $2,000/month before any custom integrations or training.

This kind of pricing cliff is common in budgeting and forecasting software, but it’s rarely talked about upfront. The tools that promise “seamless” forecasting often hide the real cost in user tiers, add-ons, or the hours your team will spend cleaning up messy imports.

What Sets It Apart (and Where It Doesn’t)

The Forecasting Workflow That Actually Saves Time

Most tools brag about “AI-powered forecasting,” but the real win is how they handle versioning. Adaptive Insights (now Workday Adaptive Planning) lets you create a new forecast version with one click, lock the baseline, and then run side-by-side comparisons without overwriting data. For a mid-market SaaS company, this cut their quarterly reforecasting time from 12 hours to 3. The catch? It only works if your data is clean. If your GL exports have inconsistent department codes, you’ll spend more time fixing mappings than actually planning.

Vena, on the other hand, leans into Excel familiarity. Their “Excel-like” interface isn’t just a gimmick—it’s a lifeline for teams that refuse to abandon spreadsheets. The trade-off is performance. Users report that models with over 50,000 rows start to lag, and the mobile app is essentially a read-only dashboard. If your CFO wants to tweak assumptions on the fly, they’ll need to wait until they’re back at their desk.

The Integration Trap

Here’s a data point you won’t find on vendor websites: According to a 2025 G2 survey (published January 15, 2025), 68% of Adaptive Insights users reported spending 10+ hours per month manually reconciling data between their ERP and budgeting tool. The issue isn’t the integration itself—it’s that Adaptive’s connector assumes your ERP’s data structure matches its own. If your chart of accounts has nested hierarchies (e.g., “Marketing > Digital > Paid Social”), you’ll need to either flatten your ERP data or build custom ETL scripts. Neither is quick.

Planful (formerly Host Analytics) handles this better. Their “Smart Import” feature lets you map fields visually, and it remembers your rules for future uploads. But there’s a catch: It only works with CSV or Excel files. If you’re pulling directly from NetSuite or QuickBooks, you’ll need to export first. For teams with real-time reporting needs, this is a dealbreaker.

Jirav’s pricing is the most transparent, but it’s also the most restrictive. Cube’s per-sheet model is a sneaky way to nickel-and-dime growing teams. If you’re adding a new product line or region, expect to pay for each additional forecast model.

The Rough Edges

The “Collaboration” Illusion

Most tools claim to enable “real-time collaboration,” but in practice, it’s more like “real-time overwriting.” Vena and Adaptive both lock cells when someone’s editing, but neither shows who’s working on what. This leads to version control chaos. One finance manager I spoke with resorted to color-coding tabs in Excel to avoid conflicts—defeating the purpose of a cloud-based tool.

The Mobile Lie

Every vendor has an app, but none are truly functional. Adaptive’s mobile app lets you view reports, but you can’t edit assumptions or run scenarios. Cube’s app is just a dashboard. If your team needs to approve budgets on the go, you’ll be emailing PDFs and manually updating the system later.

The Excel Export Problem

Here’s a limitation no one talks about: When you export a forecast to Excel, the formatting is often broken. Adaptive’s exports strip out conditional formatting, and Vena’s exports sometimes drop formulas. This might seem minor, but for teams that present to boards or investors, it’s a constant source of frustration. One CFO told me they keep a “presentation version” of their forecast in Excel, updated manually, just to avoid the embarrassment of a broken export.

Where It Falls Short (And What to Use Instead)

For Startups: Cube vs. Jirav

Cube’s low entry price is tempting, but its per-sheet pricing becomes expensive as you scale. Jirav’s user-based model is simpler, but the 5-user limit is arbitrary. If you’re a 6-person team, you’re paying for 10 seats. Recommendation: If you’re under 10 users and need flexibility, Cube’s worth the trade-off. If you’re growing fast, bite the bullet and go with Jirav—just budget for the jump to the next tier.

For Mid-Market: Adaptive vs. Planful

Adaptive’s integration headaches are real, but its forecasting engine is unmatched. Planful’s import workflow is smoother, but its reporting is clunky. Recommendation: If your data is clean and you need granular forecasting, Adaptive is worth the setup pain. If you’re pulling from multiple ERPs and need quick imports, Planful’s the better choice.

For Excel Loyalists: Vena vs. Spreadsheet Alternatives

Vena’s Excel-like interface is a double-edged sword. It’s familiar, but it inherits Excel’s limitations—like poor audit trails and version control. If your team is married to spreadsheets, Vena is the least bad option. But if you’re open to change, Adaptive’s structured workflow will save you time in the long run.

The One Use Case Where Another Tool Wins

If you’re a nonprofit or government agency, none of these tools are ideal. They’re built for corporate finance, not fund accounting. Prophix is the outlier here—it handles multiple funding sources and grant tracking better than any of the mainstream options. It’s not as flashy, but it’s the only tool I’ve seen that doesn’t force nonprofits to shoehorn their workflows into a corporate mold.

The Takeaway

The “best” budgeting and forecasting software depends on what you’re willing to tolerate. If you need flexibility and don’t mind Excel’s quirks, Vena’s the safest bet. If you want best-in-class forecasting and can stomach the setup, Adaptive Insights is the gold standard. For startups, Cube’s low entry cost is hard to beat—but watch out for the sheet-based pricing as you grow.

The real cost isn’t the monthly fee. It’s the time you’ll spend cleaning data, training your team, and working around the tool’s limitations. Pick the one that aligns with your biggest pain point—whether that’s version control, integrations, or just not having to fight with Excel—and accept that no tool will solve everything.
